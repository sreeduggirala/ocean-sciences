# Stommel 1961: The Paper That Changed Oceanography

## Henry Stommel (1920–1992)

Henry Stommel was an American physical oceanographer whose work fundamentally transformed our understanding of ocean circulation. Born in 1920, Stommel spent most of his career at the **Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution** (WHOI) on Cape Cod, Massachusetts — a setting that bridged theory, observation, and instrumentation in ways that defined modern oceanography.

### Key Career Milestones

| Year | Achievement |
|------|-------------|
| 1944 | Joins WHOI as a research assistant |
| 1958 | Publishes "The Abyssal Circulation" — demonstrates ocean currents are thermohaline-driven, not wind-driven alone |
| 1961 | **"Thermohaline Convection with Two Stable Regimes of Flow"** — *Tellus* |
| 1962 | Proposes the "Gulf Stream as a Deep Western Boundary Current" (explains western intensification) |
| 1965 | Contributes to founding the field of **paleoceanography** by linking climate oscillations to circulation changes |
| 1989 | Retires from WHOI; awarded numerous honors |
| 1992 | Dies; his legacy shapes decades of AMOC research |

### What Made Stommel Revolutionary

Before Stommel, oceanographers had two competing views of what drives ocean circulation:

1. **Wind-driven perspective**: Ekman spiral theory suggested surface winds pump water around ocean basins, and this dominates the observable currents (Gulf Stream, Kuroshio).

2. **Thermohaline perspective** (nascent): Density differences between warm and cold, salty and fresh water could drive a slow, deep circulation — but nobody had shown this systematically.

Stommel was unique in:
- **Bridging theory and observation**: He developed mathematical models that matched real observations.
- **Using simple models powerfully**: While others built complicated theories, Stommel showed that even the *simplest* dynamical system can have profound implications.
- **Demonstrating non-linearity**: The 1961 paper was one of the first to show that a physical system could have **multiple stable equilibria** and **catastrophic transitions** between them.

## The 1961 Paper: "Thermohaline Convection with Two Stable Regimes of Flow"

### What Was the Paper About?

Stommel studied a two-box ocean model: one representing the equatorial Atlantic, the other the high-latitude (polar) Atlantic. He tracked how **temperature** and **salinity** (salt concentration) in each box would evolve under different forcing conditions.

The genius was his question: *What if we increase freshwater input to the polar box (mimicking glacial melt or increased rain)? Does the circulation grow weaker gradually, or does it collapse suddenly?*

### The Shocking Discovery: Bistability

Stommel showed mathematically that the answer is **neither straightforward nor reversible**. He found:

1. **Two stable equilibria exist** under the same parameter values:
   - **Regime I**: Strong northward circulation, carrying warm, salty water to the pole (thermally driven).
   - **Regime II**: Weak or reversed circulation, with fresh, cold water stagnating at the pole (salinity-dominated).

2. **Transitions are irreversible** (hysteresis):
   - *Forward*: Gradually increase freshwater input → circulation weakens smoothly → at a critical threshold, it **collapses suddenly**.
   - *Backward*: Even if you reverse the freshwater input, the circulation does not spontaneously recover. You must reduce freshwater *below* the original level to restore it.

3. **The middle branch is unstable**: Between the two stable regimes lies an unstable equilibrium that acts as a "saddle point." Trajectories avoid it; it's invisible in long simulations but crucial to understanding the geometry of the tipping point.

### The Mathematics (Sketch)

Stommel's original (1961) two-box model uses a **relaxation-toward-equilibrium** salinity forcing rather than a constant freshwater flux:

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1)$$

$$\frac{dS_2}{dt} = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2)$$

Where $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$ is the circulation strength, $\lambda$ is the salinity relaxation rate (~1000-year timescale), and $S_i^0$ are equilibrium target salinities. (Many later papers use a simplified constant-flux variant $\dot S_e = -q\Delta S + F$; this simulator implements the relaxation form.)

At steady state ($\frac{dS_i}{dt} = 0$), the system yields a **quadratic equation** in the salinity difference:

$$2k\beta(\Delta S)^2 - (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)\Delta S + \lambda \Delta S^0 = 0$$

A quadratic can have **0, 1, or 2 real roots**:
- **2 roots** → two stable states (bistable)
- **1 root** → saddle-node bifurcation (tipping point)
- **0 roots** → no equilibrium

The discriminant $\mathcal{D} = (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0$ determines which regime:
- $\mathcal{D} > 0$: two equilibria (bistable / hysteresis region)
- $\mathcal{D} = 0$: at the saddle-node bifurcation
- $\mathcal{D} < 0$: only one equilibrium (the haline / collapsed branch)

## Why This Was Revolutionary

### 1. **First Mathematical Model of Irreversibility in Climate**

Before Stommel, most climate scientists thought of the climate system as a slowly varying, reversible pendulum. Stommel showed that under the right conditions, a climate subsystem could flip catastrophically and resist reversal. This was **radical**.

### 2. **Explained Paleoclimate Puzzles**

Geological records show that the last ice age was punctuated by episodes of abrupt warming:
- **Dansgaard-Oeschger (D-O) events**: Greenland surface temperatures would jump ~10°C within centuries, then gradually cool over millennia, then cycle again.
- **Younger Dryas**: After the last glaciation, a sudden return to glacial conditions for ~1,200 years.

Stommel's model provided a *mechanism*: freshwater from melting ice sheets could destabilize the AMOC, flipping it to a weak state. Once in that state, it would resist recovery for thousands of years until boundary conditions changed enough.

### 3. **Bridge to Dynamical Systems Theory**

The 1961 paper used concepts from **bifurcation theory** — a branch of mathematics studying how the number and stability of equilibria change with parameters. This was cutting-edge in applied mathematics (Thom, Arnold, and others were developing catastrophe theory in the 1960s). Stommel's paper showed that geophysics is a rich arena for testing these ideas.

### 4. **Predictive Value**

The model made a testable prediction: if you can measure the AMOC and track freshwater forcing, you should be able to identify whether the system is in a bistable region and estimate distance to a tipping point. This is exactly what modern observationalists (RAPID-MOCHA, OSNAP, etc.) are now attempting.

## Historical Reception and Scientific Lineage

### The Paper's Impact Timeline

| Era | Response |
|-----|----------|
| 1961–1970s | Skepticism among observationalists ("it's just a toy model") but enthusiasm among modelers |
| 1980s | Rooth (1982) extends model with realistic salinity; bifurcation analysis becomes standard |
| 1990s–2000s | Rahmstorf revives work, connects to paleoclimate; Dijkstra shows chaos; multiple coupled models confirm bistability |
| 2010s | RAPID observations suggest AMOC is **not** in a bistable region yet, but weakening |
| 2020s | Boers (2021), Weijer et al. (2020) argue AMOC may be approaching tipping; major concern for climate policy |

### The Modern Lineage

**Immediate successors**:
- **Rooth (1982)**: Refined the two-box model to include realistic surface/deep water mixing.
- **Marotzke & Willebrand (1991)**: Extended to four-box model; showed hysteresis persists in greater complexity.

**Theoretical pioneers**:
- **Rahmstorf (1996, 2002)**: Systematic bifurcation analysis of Stommel-type models; connected to paleoclimate D-O events.
- **Dijkstra & Molemaker (1997)**: Found **chaos** in three-box thermohaline models — suggesting oscillations under certain forcing.
- **Schewe & Levermann (2017)**: Mapped the parameter space of AMOC bistability in intermediate complexity models.

**Observational motivation**:
- **RAPID-MOCHA (2004–present)**: Deployed mooring array in the Atlantic measuring AMOC continuously. Mean: 17.4 ± 4.4 Sv. Decadal trends suggest weakness.
- **Caesar et al. (2018)**: Showed AMOC weakening by 15% since 1900; part of a longer-term decline.
- **Boers (2021)**: Used Atlantic sea surface temperature data to argue AMOC may be approaching a critical transition (short residence time in state space).

**Climate impact**:
- **Lenton et al. (2008)**: Identified AMOC collapse as one of nine "tipping elements" in the climate system — irreversible on human timescales if exceeded.
- **IPCC AR6 (2021)**: Assessed AMOC as "very unlikely" to collapse this century under Paris Agreement scenarios, but "collapse cannot be ruled out" under high-emission paths.

## Connecting to Modern Climate Science

### Why Stommel Still Matters

1. **AMOC is weakening**: The Atlantic circulation has declined by ~15% since 1900 (Caesar et al. 2018). We don't know if we're approaching a tipping point, but Stommel's model tells us what collapse would look like if it happens.

2. **Hysteresis has policy implications**: If AMOC is bistable and we're in the hysteresis region, then merely stopping CO₂ emissions might not be enough — we might need to actively cool the planet to recover it.

3. **Paleoclimate oscillations repeat the pattern**: The D-O events and Younger Dryas look exactly like Stommel's predicted transitions. This suggests the two-box model captures something fundamental about the ocean's dynamics.

4. **Simplified models still rule**: Despite 65 years of advances, the two-box model remains one of the best teaching and diagnostic tools in oceanography. Its simplicity is its strength.
