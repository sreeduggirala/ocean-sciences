# Ocean Physics Background

## What Is the Thermohaline Circulation?

The **thermohaline circulation (THC)**, often called the "ocean conveyor belt," is a global system of interconnected currents driven by differences in **temperature** (thermal) and **salinity** (haline). Unlike wind-driven surface currents (Gulf Stream, Kuroshio), the THC operates on timescales of centuries to millennia and directly connects the surface, intermediate, and abyssal ocean.

### The Conveyor Belt Picture

Imagine a giant loop:

1. **Sinking zone (North Atlantic)**: Cold, salty water at high latitudes becomes denser and sinks to the ocean floor.
2. **Deep flow (southward & globally)**: This dense water spreads south and eventually circulates globally at great depth.
3. **Upwelling zones (Southern Ocean, tropics)**: Deep water gradually rises to the surface, driven by wind and mixing.
4. **Surface return (northward in Atlantic)**: Warm surface water flows from the tropics back to the North Atlantic, completing the loop.

The round-trip takes **~1000 years**. This slow circulation is why the THC is such a powerful heat pump: it continuously carries tropical heat toward the poles.

## Density Drives Circulation

The fundamental principle: **Water moves from high-pressure (dense) to low-pressure (light) regions**. In the ocean:

$$\rho = \rho_0(1 - \alpha T + \beta S)$$

Where:
- **ρ**: density (kg/m³)
- **ρ₀**: reference density (~1025 kg/m³ for seawater)
- **T**: in-situ temperature (°C)
- **S**: salinity (psu, practical salinity units)
- **α ≈ 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ /°C**: **thermal expansion coefficient** (this is the key parameter in our model)
- **β ≈ 8 × 10⁻⁴ /psu**: **haline contraction coefficient**

This is a **linear equation of state** — a simplification, but accurate for the range of temperatures and salinities in the deep ocean (0–30°C, 34–36 psu).

## The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)

The **Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)** is the Atlantic branch of the global THC. It is the most closely observed and modeled component of the thermohaline circulation, partly because:

1. It transports so much heat.
2. It directly affects European and North American climate.
3. We have continuous measurements from the RAPID-MOCHA array since 2004.

### AMOC Structure

**North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW) Formation**:
- In winter, over the **Labrador Sea** (southeast of Greenland) and **Nordic Seas** (between Greenland, Iceland, and Norway), surface water becomes very cold and salty (via sea ice formation, which expels salt).
- This dense water sinks to abyssal depths (~3000–4000 m).
- ~17 Sv (Sverdrups; 1 Sv = 10⁶ m³/s) of deep water form this way annually on average.

**Southward deep flow**:
- NADW flows southward through the Atlantic basin at depth.

**Upwelling and return**:
- In the Southern Ocean and tropics, this deep water gradually rises (via wind-driven upwelling and turbulent mixing).
- Warm surface water from the tropics flows northward to replace the sinking water.
- This warm, northward-flowing surface current is what we see as the Gulf Stream and its continuation, the North Atlantic Current.

### AMOC Strength: Observations

The **RAPID-MOCHA array** has measured AMOC continuously since April 2004 with moorings across the Atlantic at 26°N:

$$\text{AMOC} = 16.9 \pm 4.4 \text{ Sv (mean ± standard deviation, 2004--2020)}$$

This mean of **~17 Sv** is what we calibrate our simulator's default parameters to match.

**Recent trends**:
- Caesar et al. (2018): AMOC has weakened by ~15% since 1900 (using paleo sea surface temperature and salinity proxies combined with recent observations).
- Boers (2021): Analysis of Atlantic sea surface temperatures suggests AMOC may be moving toward a critical transition.
- Persistent decline: The RAPID array itself shows no significant trend (2004–2020), but longer-timescale paleo evidence is concerning.

## The Two Competing Feedbacks

Stommel's genius was recognizing that temperature and salinity represent **two antagonistic feedback loops** in the North Atlantic.

### Positive Feedback (Thermal Feedback — Stabilizing)

$$\text{Warm equatorial water} \to \text{Northward transport} \to \text{Polar warming} \to \text{Higher salinity from evaporation} \to \text{Denser water} \to \text{Stronger sinking} \to \text{Stronger circulation}$$

**The loop**:
1. The equator is warm (T_e = 25°C), the pole is cold (T_p = 5°C).
2. This temperature contrast drives a density gradient that pulls warm surface water northward.
3. As warm water moves north, it transports heat and **salt** (since warm water at the equator is salty).
4. At the pole, this salt-enriched water sinks, strengthening the circulation.
5. Stronger circulation means more salt transport north → more sinking → **self-reinforcing** (positive feedback in the sense of reinforcement, but *stabilizing* because it resists perturbations).

**Why it's stabilizing**: If the circulation weakens for any reason, less salt reaches the pole, making the pole fresher and less dense, which *further* weakens sinking. But this reduction in sinking means less heat is removed from the tropics, so the equator warms and the pole cools, *restoring* the thermal gradient and the circulation. The system resists the perturbation.

### Negative Feedback (Haline Feedback — Destabilizing)

$$\text{Freshwater input at pole} \to \text{Polar freshening} \to \text{Reduced density} \to \text{Weaker sinking} \to \text{Weaker circulation} \to \text{Less salt northward} \to \text{Even fresher pole}$$

**The loop**:
1. Freshwater is input to the polar box (glacial melt, excess precipitation, melting sea ice).
2. This freshens (dilutes) the polar surface water.
3. Fresher water is less dense, so it floats — sinking is suppressed.
4. Weaker sinking means weaker circulation → less warm, salty water transported north.
5. Less salt input to the pole makes it even fresher → even weaker sinking.
6. This is a **positive feedback loop** (amplifies the perturbation), and it destabilizes the circulation.

**Why it's destabilizing**: Once freshwater starts to accumulate at the pole, the feedback loop *amplifies* the effect, potentially leading to a runaway collapse.

## Freshwater Forcing: What It Represents

In the simulator, the **freshwater flux F** is a control parameter representing:

| Physical Process | What It Models |
|------------------|----------------|
| **Glacial melt** | Greenland Ice Sheet discharge (particularly since 1980s, accelerating) |
| **Sea ice formation/melting** | Net freshwater from the ice-albedo feedback |
| **Precipitation minus evaporation (P-E)** | Net moisture convergence at high latitudes |
| **River discharge** | Arctic river runoff and estuarine freshwater flux |

Historically, at the end of the last ice age (11,700 years ago), massive freshwater influx from melting ice sheets and Lake Agassiz drainage are thought to have **triggered** (or at least amplified) the Younger Dryas cooling event — a sudden return to near-glacial conditions for ~1,200 years.

In today's context:
- Greenland ice sheet mass loss has accelerated from ~30 Gt/year (1990s) to ~280 Gt/year (2010s).
- Arctic sea ice is declining rapidly, changing freshwater distribution.
- If AMOC is in a bistable region, these freshwater changes could push it toward collapse.

## The Density Difference Equation

At the heart of the simulator is a simplified expression for how the **density difference** drives circulation:

$$\frac{\Delta \rho}{\rho_0} = -\alpha \Delta T + \beta \Delta S$$

Where:
- $\Delta T = T_e - T_p$ (equatorial minus polar; always positive in our setup)
- $\Delta S = S_e - S_p$ (equatorial minus polar salinity difference)

**Interpretation**:
- When $\Delta \rho > 0$ (equatorial water is denser): Paradoxically, *lighter* polar water sinks and *heavier* equatorial water rises — no, wait. Actually, we're comparing the *same density reference*. The density difference drives a pressure gradient in the ocean interior.
- More carefully: **Large positive $\Delta \rho$** means equatorial water is denser, creating a pressure head that drives *surface* flow northward (and deep southward return).
- When $\Delta S$ is large and positive (equator much saltier than pole), $\beta \Delta S$ term is large, which reduces the net density difference → weaker circulation.
- When $\Delta S$ becomes negative (pole saltier than equator!), the $-\beta \Delta S$ term reverses, aiding thermal forcing — but in that regime, the circulation has typically collapsed anyway.

## From Density to Circulation

The model assumes that circulation strength instantly equilibrates to the density difference (no inertia):

$$q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$$

Where:
- **q** (1/s): dimensionless circulation strength
- **k ≈ 1.5 × 10⁻⁶ 1/s**: empirical circulation coefficient
- **[α ΔT - β ΔS]**: the net density difference normalized

**Physical interpretation**:
- $q > 0$ (positive): strong northward circulation (AMOC-like)
- $q ≈ 0$ (near zero): circulation is very weak (collapsed)
- $q < 0$ (negative): reversed circulation (haline-dominated, polar-to-equatorial surface flow) — rare but possible in the model

**Conversion to Sverdrups**:
To compare with observations, we convert from 1/s to Sverdrups using the box volume:

$$q_{Sv} = q \cdot V_{\text{box}} / 10^6$$

With typical box volumes of ~7×10¹⁵ m³, this scaling gives default model parameters yielding ~14.7 Sv, close to the observed mean of 16.9 Sv.

## Real AMOC vs. The Model

### What the Model Captures

✅ The **bistability** inherent in density-driven circulation
✅ The **multiple equilibria** and **hysteresis** observed in paleoclimate
✅ The role of freshwater forcing as a **tipping mechanism**
✅ The **timescale** of transitions (centennial to millennial)

### What the Model Omits

❌ Wind forcing (important for AMOC but secondary for tipping)
❌ Nutrient cycling and biological feedbacks
❌ Bathymetry and continental geometry
❌ Eddies and fine-scale mixing
❌ Interactions with the cryosphere (sea ice, ice sheets) beyond freshwater
❌ Coupled atmosphere-ocean feedbacks

The model is a **caricature**, but a powerful one. By stripping away complexity, Stommel revealed the essential nonlinear dynamics.

## Observing the THC

### RAPID-MOCHA Array

Since 2004, the UK/US RAPID-MOCHA program has maintained a mooring array at 26°N across the Atlantic:
- **26°N location**: Chosen because west of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the interior ocean is well-mixed vertically; east of the ridge, upwelling and boundary currents are better resolved.
- **Moorings**: Measure velocity, temperature, and salinity at multiple depths.
- **AMOC strength**: Computed as the net mass transport poleward minus equatorward.
- **Variability**: Measured AMOC fluctuates on weekly to interannual timescales; the 2004–2020 mean is 16.9 Sv with a standard deviation of 4.4 Sv.

### Paleoclimate Proxies

Before modern measurements, we infer AMOC strength from:
- **Radiocarbon dating** of benthic foraminifera (relates to deep water age).
- **Isotope ratios** in sediment cores (temperature and salinity fingerprints).
- **Sea surface temperature (SST)** patterns (Atlantic Meridional Temperature Gradient strongly linked to AMOC).

## Why This Matters for Climate

1. **Heat transport**: The AMOC carries ~1.3 petawatts of heat northward. If it weakens significantly, Northern Europe cools, sea level rises in the Western Atlantic, and global precipitation patterns shift.

2. **Carbon cycle**: The AMOC drives the "biological pump" — nutrient upwelling supports productive fisheries and draws down atmospheric CO₂.

3. **Irreversibility**: Hysteresis in the AMOC means that once collapsed, recovery is not automatic. This has profound implications for long-term climate policy.

4. **Regional vs. global**: An AMOC collapse doesn't mean global climate chaos — most models show modest global warming but regional cooling in the North Atlantic. But the regional impacts (European agriculture, North Atlantic fisheries) could be severe.
