# Bifurcations, Tipping Points, and Hysteresis

## Bifurcation: A Change in System Behavior

A **bifurcation** occurs when the number or stability of equilibria changes as a parameter varies. In the Stommel model, as freshwater forcing **F** increases, the system undergoes a **saddle-node (fold) bifurcation** — the defining feature of tipping points and hysteresis.

---

## The S-Curve and Fold Bifurcation

When we plot the equilibrium circulation strength **q** against the bifurcation parameter **F**, we get the characteristic **S-shaped curve**:

```
q (circulation strength, Sv)
 ^
 |    ______ (upper branch, stable AMOC)
 |   /
 |  |        (middle branch, unstable)
 |   \_____
 |         \______ (lower branch, weak/collapsed)
 |
 +-------+-------+------+-----> F (freshwater flux)
      F_c2    F_c1   F_crit
         ↑       ↑
      (backward) (forward)
      recovery   collapse
        point    point
```

### Three Regimes

1. **For F < F_c2** (low freshwater):
   - Only one stable equilibrium: the upper branch (strong AMOC)
   - System stays in strong state

2. **For F_c2 < F < F_c1** (intermediate, bistable regime):
   - **TWO** stable equilibria coexist (upper and lower branches)
   - **Hysteresis region**: Which state you're in depends on history
   - An unstable middle branch separates the two basins of attraction

3. **For F > F_c1** (high freshwater):
   - Only one stable equilibrium: the lower branch (weak/collapsed AMOC)
   - System must collapse; no stable strong state exists

---

## Forward Tipping: Collapse

**Scenario**: Gradually increase freshwater forcing (e.g., Greenland melt).

**Dynamics**:

1. **Initial state**: System is on the upper (strong AMOC) branch
   - q ≈ 15 Sv (strong northward circulation)

2. **Slow increase of F**:
   - Equilibrium moves along the upper branch toward the fold
   - q decreases gradually (circulation weakens)
   - But the system remains stable; perturbations decay

3. **Approaching F_c1** (forward tipping point):
   - Equilibrium q approaches the fold point
   - Stability eigenvalue λ₂ → 0⁻ (marginal stability)
   - System becomes increasingly "fragile": recovery from perturbations is slow

4. **F exceeds F_c1**:
   - Upper branch equilibrium no longer exists
   - The fold has been crossed
   - System **cannot stay** on upper branch

5. **Collapse**:
   - Trajectory jumps discontinuously from upper branch to lower branch
   - q drops rapidly from ~15 Sv to near 0 (or reverses)
   - This is the **tipping event**: a sudden, catastrophic transition

6. **Post-collapse state**:
   - Now on the lower (weak/reversed) branch
   - q ≈ 0–5 Sv or even negative
   - Polar box becomes fresher (less salty) due to weak northward salt transport

### Key Feature: Irreversibility Forward Path Cannot Be Retraced

Once you've tipped, simply **reversing** the forcing does not restore the original state — this is hysteresis.

---

## Backward Recovery: The Hysteresis Loop

**Scenario**: After collapse, gradually *reduce* freshwater forcing.

**Dynamics**:

1. **Start on lower branch** with high F (post-collapse):
   - q is weak or reversed

2. **Decrease F slowly**:
   - Equilibrium moves along the lower branch
   - q remains weak; the system does not spontaneously recover
   - The upper branch does not reappear yet

3. **Continue decreasing F** past the original collapse point F_c1:
   - Still no recovery! The lower branch is still stable
   - The system "remembers" its collapsed state

4. **Approaching F_c2** (backward recovery threshold):
   - Lower branch equilibrium approaches its fold point
   - Stability eigenvalue λ₂ → 0⁻ again

5. **F drops below F_c2**:
   - Lower branch equilibrium vanishes
   - System **cannot stay** on lower branch

6. **Recovery jump**:
   - Trajectory jumps from lower branch back to upper branch
   - q suddenly increases to ~15 Sv
   - Circulation is restored

### The Hysteresis Window

$$\text{Hysteresis width} = F_{c1} - F_{c2}$$

Example with default parameters:
- $F_{c1} \approx 2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ psu/s (collapse threshold)
- $F_{c2} \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-4}$ psu/s (recovery threshold)
- Hysteresis width ≈ $1.0 \times 10^{-4}$ psu/s

**Physical interpretation**: In the hysteresis window, the system "sticks" to whichever state it entered first. Reversing the cause (F) is insufficient to reverse the effect (AMOC state).

## Why Hysteresis Matters for Climate Policy

### The Tipping Point Dilemma

If Earth's AMOC is currently in the bistable region (some evidence suggests it might be), then:

1. **If we reach F_c1**: AMOC will collapse even if we stabilize forcing at that level.
2. **If we exceed F_c1 and then reduce forcing**: We must reduce all the way to F_c2 (much lower) to restore AMOC.
3. **The cost of delay**: Every decade without reducing F increases the threshold we must overshoot to eventually recover.

### Paleoclimate Analogue: The Younger Dryas

The **Younger Dryas** (12,900–11,700 years ago) is a dramatic example:

- **Event**: After the last glaciation's warming trend, temperatures suddenly *dropped* back to near-glacial levels for ~1,200 years
- **Suspected trigger**: Massive freshwater discharge from the melting North American ice sheet (Laurentide Ice Sheet collapse) into the Atlantic
- **AMOC mechanism**: Freshwater input exceeded a critical threshold, collapsing the AMOC → reduced northward heat transport → rapid cooling over Europe and North Atlantic
- **Recovery**: Gradual increase in salinity (as freshwater input ceased) allowed AMOC to recover after ~1,200 years

The Younger Dryas shows that:
- **Hysteresis is real** in Earth's climate system
- **Abrupt reversals are possible** on century timescales
- **The ocean's memory** (in salinity structure) can trap the climate in an unfavorable state for centuries

## Bifurcation Diagram: Reading and Interpreting

### Bifurcation Diagram in the Simulator

When you click **"Compute Bifurcation"**, the simulator sweeps F from minimum to maximum in both directions:

```
Forward sweep (F increasing):
    Start at F_min, find stable equilibria as F → F_max
    Trace the upper branch until F_c1, then jump to lower branch

Backward sweep (F decreasing):
    Start at F_max, find stable equilibria as F → F_min
    Trace the lower branch until F_c2, then jump to upper branch
```

### Visual Elements

- **Blue upper branch**: Stable AMOC states; plotted from forward sweep
- **Orange lower branch**: Stable weak/collapsed states; plotted from backward sweep
- **Dashed grey middle branch**: Unstable saddle points (inferred from quadratic formula)
- **Red diamonds**: Tipping points (F_c1 and F_c2)
- **Orange shaded region**: Bistable (hysteresis) region
- **Orange X marker**: Current F value (from slider); shows where you are on the diagram

### How to Use It

1. **Identify hysteresis window**: Look for the gap between the forward and backward curves. The width of the gap is the hysteresis width.
2. **Locate current state**: The orange X shows where your current F falls. Is it in the bistable region?
3. **Estimate stability**: If you're in the monostable region (single stable branch), perturbations may not flip the system. In the bistable region, noise can tip you over.
4. **Plan mitigation**: If F is approaching F_c1, reducing freshwater forcing becomes urgent to avoid collapse.

## Saddle-Node Bifurcation: The Mathematics

A **saddle-node bifurcation** (also called a fold bifurcation) is a local bifurcation where two fixed points collide and annihilate as a parameter varies.

### Normal Form

Near a saddle-node bifurcation, the dynamics can be reduced to the 1D "normal form":

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = \mu + x^2$$

where:
- $x$ represents the deviation of the state from the bifurcation
- $\mu$ is the bifurcation parameter (shifted so $\mu = 0$ at the bifurcation)

**Behavior**:
- For $\mu < 0$: Two equilibria exist at $x = \pm\sqrt{-\mu}$ (one stable, one unstable)
- For $\mu = 0$: One equilibrium at $x = 0$ (saddle-node)
- For $\mu > 0$: No equilibrium; trajectories escape to infinity

This is exactly the behavior of the Stommel model's S-curve!

### Stability Near Bifurcation

At a saddle-node bifurcation:
- The stable branch's eigenvalue λ₂ → 0⁻ (marginal stability)
- Perturbation recovery time $\tau = -1/\lambda_2 \to \infty$ (critical slowing down)
- System becomes increasingly sluggish and sensitive to noise

This is why **early-warning signals** of tipping can sometimes be detected: increased variability and autocorrelation in time series before a bifurcation.

---

## Stochastic Tipping

Without noise, the deterministic forward and backward thresholds are F_c1 and F_c2. But add stochastic forcing:

$$\frac{dS_e}{dt} = -q(S_e - S_p) + F + \xi(t)$$

$$\frac{dS_p}{dt} = +q(S_e - S_p) - F + \xi(t)$$

where $\xi(t)$ is Gaussian white noise.

### Noise-Induced Transitions

1. **Noise can trigger early tipping**: Even if F < F_c1, a large stochastic fluctuation can kick the system across the unstable manifold, causing it to escape to the lower branch.

2. **Noise can accelerate recovery**: With noise, recovery from the lower branch can occur before deterministically reaching F_c2.

3. **Mean transition time**: Depends on noise intensity and distance from the bifurcation. Very close to bifurcation, even tiny noise can cause occasional transitions.

### In the Simulator

The **noise_amplitude** slider lets you:
- Set noise = 0: See purely deterministic behavior
- Increase noise: Watch occasional transitions appear before reaching the deterministic tipping point
- Compare: Run the same parameters multiple times with noise; trajectories diverge, showing stochasticity

This demonstrates that the real climate system, with weather noise and natural variability, may be more fragile than deterministic models suggest.

## Paleoclimate Oscillations: D-O Events

**Dansgaard-Oeschger (D-O) events** are rapid climate oscillations visible in Greenland ice cores during the last glaciation:

- **Duration**: ~1,500–10,000 years each
- **Temperature jump**: ~10°C warming in a few decades over Greenland
- **Frequency**: Roughly every 1,500 years
- **Cooling tail**: Gradual cooling back to glacial baseline after warming

### AMOC Explanation (Stommel-Type Mechanism)

Paleoceanographers propose that D-O events involve AMOC oscillations:

1. **Warm mode**: AMOC is strong, carrying heat northward → Greenland warms by ~10°C relative to baseline
2. **Transition**: Freshwater buildup (possibly from ice sheet surge) increases F
3. **Collapse**: AMOC weakens and potentially reverses → heat transport drops → Greenland rapidly cools
4. **Recovery**: Freshwater slowly decreases or salinity restabilizes → AMOC recovers → cycle repeats

This is **not quite hysteresis** in the Stommel two-box sense (which is static), but rather a **dynamic oscillation** around bistable thresholds, possibly driven by ice sheet feedbacks and stochastic variability.

## Critical Slowing Down: An Early-Warning Signal

As the system approaches a bifurcation, its response to perturbations slows:

$$\tau = -\frac{1}{\lambda_2} \to \infty \text{ as } \lambda_2 \to 0$$

This **critical slowing down** has consequences:

1. **Increased autocorrelation**: Time series becomes more "red" (power concentrated at low frequencies)
2. **Increased variance**: System fluctuates more in response to noise
3. **Slower recovery**: Perturbations take longer to decay

### Observable Precursors

In paleoclimate or observational data, we can sometimes detect:
- Rising autocorrelation of temperature or salinity time series
- Increasing standard deviation of fluctuations
- Spectral shifts toward lower frequencies

These are the **canaries in the coal mine** — early warning that the system is approaching a bifurcation.

---

## Can We Detect AMOC Approaching a Tipping Point?

This is an **active research question**. Recent work:

- **Boers (2021)**: Analyzed Atlantic sea surface temperature anomalies and found indicators of critical slowing down, suggesting AMOC may be approaching a transition. *But*: Statistical significance is debated.
- **RAPID-MOCHA data**: No definitive trend over 2004–2020, though decadal variations are significant.
- **Paleoclimate**: Longer-term paleo records suggest AMOC has weakened ~15% since 1900 (Caesar et al. 2018).

### Consensus

- AMOC is **likely** in the bistable region (theory predicts it is)
- AMOC is **probably** weakening (observations suggest it)
- Whether it is **near a tipping point** is uncertain (estimates range from "very unlikely this century" to "possible by 2100")

This uncertainty underscores the importance of models like Stommel's: they help us understand the *mechanism* and *potential* for tipping, even if exact timing remains elusive.
