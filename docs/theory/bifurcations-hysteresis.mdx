# Bifurcations, Tipping Points, and Hysteresis

## Bifurcation: A Change in System Behavior

A **bifurcation** occurs when the number or stability of equilibria changes as a parameter varies. In the **original Stommel model with relaxation forcing**, the key bifurcation parameter is the **temperature difference ΔT = T₁ − T₂**. As ΔT *decreases* (due to cooling of polar regions or warming of the equator), the system undergoes a **saddle-node (fold) bifurcation** — the defining feature of tipping points in thermohaline circulation.

---

## The S-Curve and Fold Bifurcation

When we plot the equilibrium circulation strength **q** against the **temperature difference ΔT**, we get the characteristic **S-shaped curve**:

```
q (circulation strength, 1/s)
 ^
 |            _______ (upper branch, stable AMOC)
 |           /
 |          /
 |  RED •  / (bifurcation point at ΔT_crit)
 |   _____
 |  /
 | /
 +------+----------+---> ΔT (K)
     ΔT_crit   ΔT_obs
    (collapse) (today)

    (lower branch, stable collapsed state, always present)
```

### Three Regimes

1. **For ΔT > ΔT_crit** (large temperature difference, like present-day):
   - **TWO** stable equilibria coexist (upper and lower branches)
   - Upper branch: strong thermally-driven AMOC
   - Lower branch: weak haline-dominated collapsed state
   - **Bistable region**: Which state you're in depends on paleoclimate history
   - An unstable saddle-node (middle branch) separates the two basins

2. **For ΔT = ΔT_crit** (critical temperature):
   - One equilibrium at the saddle-node bifurcation
   - Marginal stability; critical slowing down

3. **For ΔT < ΔT_crit** (small temperature difference, glacial-like):
   - Only one stable equilibrium: the lower branch (weak/collapsed AMOC)
   - Upper branch no longer exists
   - System is forced into collapsed state

---

## Forward Tipping: Collapse (ΔT Decreases)

**Scenario**: Gradually decrease temperature difference ΔT (e.g., polar amplification, tropical warming, or ice age onset).

**Dynamics**:

1. **Initial state**: System is on the upper (strong AMOC) branch
   - q ≈ 5-10 × 10⁻⁷ (1/s) [~15–20 Sv in realistic units]
   - ΔT ≈ 20°C (present-day Earth)

2. **Slow decrease of ΔT** (e.g., polar region cooling faster than equator, or equatorial warming):
   - Equilibrium moves along the upper branch toward the fold
   - q decreases gradually (circulation weakens)
   - System remains stable; perturbations decay

3. **Approaching ΔT_crit** (critical temperature difference):
   - Equilibrium q approaches the fold point
   - Stability eigenvalue $\mu_1 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^* \to 0^-$ (marginal stability)
   - System becomes increasingly "fragile": critical slowing down
   - Recovery from perturbations becomes very slow (~centuries)

4. **ΔT drops below ΔT_crit**:
   - Upper branch equilibrium no longer exists
   - The fold has been crossed
   - System **cannot stay** on upper branch

5. **Collapse**:
   - Trajectory jumps discontinuously from upper branch to lower branch
   - q drops rapidly toward 0 or reverses sign
   - **This is the tipping event**: a sudden, catastrophic transition
   - Happens on timescales of ~50–500 years depending on system parameters

6. **Post-collapse state**:
   - Now on the lower (weak/reversed) branch with ΔT < ΔT_crit
   - q ≈ 0 or negative (weak or reversed circulation)
   - Polar box becomes fresh; equatorial box becomes salty
   - Heat transport to high latitudes drops → rapid cooling over decades

### Key Feature: Irreversibility—Temperature Cannot Be Easily Reversed

Once you've tipped, simply **increasing ΔT back** to its original value does NOT restore the original state. This is the essence of **hysteresis**. Because ΔT is set by long-term climate (not easily reversed), recovery requires sustained warming to increase ΔT above ΔT_crit again.

---

## Backward Recovery: Temperature Must Rise to Escape Collapse

**Scenario**: After collapse (system now on lower branch with ΔT < ΔT_crit), gradually *increase* ΔT (e.g., global warming).

**Dynamics**:

1. **Start on lower branch** with ΔT < ΔT_crit (post-collapse):
   - q is weak or reversed
   - System is trapped in collapsed state

2. **Increase ΔT slowly**:
   - Equilibrium moves along the lower branch (both branches don't exist yet)
   - q remains weak; the system does not spontaneously recover
   - Upper branch does not reappear until ΔT reaches ΔT_crit again

3. **Continue increasing ΔT** — in fact, you MUST increase ΔT all the way to **ΔT_crit** to escape:
   - Below ΔT_crit: only lower branch exists (stable)
   - System "remembers" its collapsed state

4. **ΔT approaches ΔT_crit** (recovery threshold):
   - Lower branch equilibrium approaches its fold point
   - Stability eigenvalue $\mu_1 \to 0^-$ again (critical slowing down)

5. **ΔT increases past ΔT_crit**:
   - Upper branch suddenly reappears
   - System now has a choice between two stable branches

6. **Recovery jump** (if noise or transient kicks system):
   - Trajectory can jump from lower branch back to upper branch
   - q suddenly increases
   - Circulation is restored to strong state

### The **No Hysteresis** Feature (Key Difference from Simplified Models)

**In the original Stommel model**, there is **NO hysteresis window** in the constant-temperature sense:
- **Collapse occurs at**: ΔT_crit (when decreasing ΔT)
- **Recovery occurs at**: ΔT_crit (when increasing ΔT back)
- **Same threshold**: Both occur at the same ΔT

This differs fundamentally from the **constant-F models** that show hysteresis in F (F_c1 ≠ F_c2).

**Physical interpretation**: In the relaxation model, the bistable region is created by the competition between thermal and haline feedbacks at fixed ΔT. As ΔT changes, the bistable region itself shifts. Recovery simply requires ΔT to return above the critical value.

**Paleoclimate implication**: AMOC collapse and recovery are **not** separated by a "hysteresis gap" in temperature. Rather, they occur at the same critical ΔT threshold. This makes the model consistent with paleoclimate observations where AMOC toggling occurs during stable periods (via noise-induced transitions) or sharply during climate shifts (forced by rapid ΔT changes).

## Temperature Control and Tipping Risk

### The Critical Question: How Close Are We to ΔT_crit?

In the original Stommel model, AMOC stability is fundamentally controlled by the **temperature difference ΔT**. Today:

- **Present-day ΔT ≈ 20°C** (equator 25°C, polar 5°C)
- **Estimated ΔT_crit ≈ 10–15°C** (from Stommel's parameters)
- **Safety margin**: ~5–10°C above critical threshold

However, climate projections show:
1. **Polar amplification**: High latitudes warm faster than tropics → ΔT could decrease
2. **Equatorial convection**: Deep convection in tropics could warm faster → ΔT decreases
3. **Risk timeline**: By 2100, some models project ΔT could drop by 2–4°C toward the critical range

### Paleoclimate Analogue: The Younger Dryas and Temperature Shifts

The **Younger Dryas** (12,900–11,700 years ago) provides a natural laboratory:

- **Context**: Northern Hemisphere was in rapid deglaciation (~0.5°C warming per century)
- **Event**: Abrupt climate reversal: temperatures dropped ~5°C back toward glacial values for ~1,200 years
- **Physical trigger**: Two factors likely converged:
  1. Massive freshwater discharge from Laurentide Ice Sheet collapse (modified oceanic properties, especially salinity)
  2. **Concurrent shift in ΔT**: With polar regions cooling (meltwater), the interhemispheric temperature gradient likely shifted toward smaller values
- **AMOC collapse**: The combination of freshwater and reduced ΔT potentially crossed the bifurcation threshold
- **Recovery**: Over centuries, freshwater input decreased and polar warming resumed → ΔT increased back above ΔT_crit → AMOC reinvigorated

The Younger Dryas demonstrates:
- **Temperature gradients matter critically**: Abrupt cooling at high latitudes is a major risk for AMOC collapse
- **Rapid transitions are possible**: AMOC can weaken and collapse within 50–500 years
- **Prolonged recovery**: Even after freshwater forcing reversed, AMOC took ~1,200 years to fully recover (long relaxation timescale)

## Bifurcation Diagram: Reading and Interpreting

### Bifurcation Diagram in the Simulator (ΔT Parameterization)

When you click **"Compute Bifurcation"**, the simulator sweeps **T₂ (polar temperature)** to vary **ΔT = T₁ − T₂** from maximum to minimum:

```
Forward sweep (T₂ decreasing, ΔT increasing):
    Start at T₂_max (low ΔT), find stable equilibria as T₂ → T₂_min
    Trace the upper and lower branches as ΔT increases
    Both branches are stable for ΔT > ΔT_crit (bistable region)

Backward sweep (T₂ increasing, ΔT decreasing):
    Start at T₂_min (high ΔT), find stable equilibria as T₂ → T₂_max
    Trace back along the same branches
    Bifurcation occurs at ΔT_crit where branches meet (saddle-node)
```

### Visual Elements

- **Blue upper branch**: Stable thermally-driven AMOC; exists for ΔT > ΔT_crit
- **Orange lower branch**: Stable haline-dominated/collapsed state; exists for all ΔT
- **Dashed grey middle branch** (rarely visible): Unstable saddle-node path (inferred from quadratic formula)
- **Red diamond**: Bifurcation point where upper and lower branches meet at ΔT = ΔT_crit
- **Blue shaded region**: Bistable region (ΔT > ΔT_crit)
- **Blue triangle or X marker**: Current ΔT = T₁ − T₂ (from sliders); shows where you are on the diagram

### How to Use It

1. **Find the critical point**: Locate the red diamond (bifurcation point) on the diagram. The ΔT-value at this point is ΔT_crit.
2. **Assess safety margin**: Compare current ΔT (blue X) to ΔT_crit. If ΔT is only 5–10°C above ΔT_crit, the system is vulnerable.
3. **Understand bistability**: For ΔT > ΔT_crit, both branches are stable. Which state AMOC occupies depends on paleoclimate history and noise.
4. **Simulate cooling**: Decrease T₂ (increase ΔT) to move right on the diagram. Increase T₂ (decrease ΔT) to move left. Watch how the equilibrium moves along the branches.
5. **Predict collapse risk**: As you decrease ΔT toward ΔT_crit, the upper branch weakens and approaches the bifurcation (critical slowing down). Below ΔT_crit, only the lower branch exists.

## Saddle-Node Bifurcation: The Mathematics

A **saddle-node bifurcation** (also called a fold bifurcation) is a local bifurcation where the upper and lower branches of the equilibrium curve collide and annihilate as a parameter (ΔT) varies.

### Geometry in the Stommel Model

In the (ΔT, q) phase space:
- **For ΔT > ΔT_crit**: The quadratic equilibrium equation has two real roots → two stable branches (plus an unstable middle branch)
- **For ΔT = ΔT_crit**: The quadratic has one repeated root → one branch (saddle-node)
- **For ΔT < ΔT_crit**: The quadratic has no real roots → no upper branch exists

The discriminant condition is:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0 = 0$$

### Normal Form Near Bifurcation

Near ΔT = ΔT_crit, we can expand the equation to get:

$$2k\beta (\Delta S)^2 - (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)(\Delta S) + \lambda\Delta S^0 \approx 0$$

The distance from bifurcation is measured by:

$$\epsilon = \frac{\Delta T - \Delta T_{\text{crit}}}{\Delta T_{\text{crit}}}$$

The two branch equilibria scale as:

$$(\Delta S)_{\pm} \propto \sqrt{\epsilon}$$

### Stability Near Bifurcation

At the saddle-node bifurcation:
- The stable branch's eigenvalue $\mu_1 \to 0^-$ (marginal stability)
- Perturbation recovery time $\tau = -1/\mu_1 \to \infty$ (critical slowing down)
- System becomes increasingly sluggish and sensitive to noise
- Variance of fluctuations increases (incr autocorrelation)

This is why **early-warning signals** of tipping can sometimes be detected: as ΔT approaches ΔT_crit, time series show increased variability, autocorrelation, and spectral reddening.

## Stochastic Tipping and Noise-Induced Transitions

Without noise, the deterministic collapse occurs exactly at ΔT = ΔT_crit. But Earth's climate includes significant natural variability (stochastic forcing):

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1) + \xi_1(t)$$

$$\frac{dS_2}{dt} = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2) + \xi_2(t)$$

where $\xi_i(t)$ represent stochastic fluctuations in salinity (due to weather, eddy variability, etc.).

### Noise-Induced Transitions (Bistable Region)

**In the bistable region** (ΔT > ΔT_crit, two stable branches coexist):

1. **Noise can trigger spontaneous transitions**: Even if ΔT is well above ΔT_crit, a large stochastic excursion can temporarily kick the system across the unstable manifold → AMOC collapses without any change in ΔT
   - This is **spontaneous tipping** or **stochastic tipping**
   - More likely when ΔT is close to ΔT_crit (critical slowing down makes noise more effective)

2. **Mean residence time in collapsed state**: Depends on noise intensity and distance from bifurcation
   - Close to bifurcation: Even tiny noise causes occasional transitions
   - Far from bifurcation: Transitions are rare

3. **Paleoclimate application**: Dansgaard-Oeschger events (D-O) cycles during glacials may represent stochastic toggling between upper and lower branches

### Noise Acceleration of Deterministic Tipping (Approaching Threshold)

**Near ΔT_crit**:
- Deterministic tipping occurs at ΔT_crit
- Noise advances the tipping point: tipping can occur at ΔT > ΔT_crit (still above threshold)
- Stochastic tipping time = **deterministic time + noise-shortened time**

### In the Simulator

The **noise_amplitude** slider lets you:
- Set noise = 0: See purely deterministic behavior; AMOC collapses only at/below ΔT_crit
- Increase noise: Watch spontaneous transitions appear even in the bistable region (ΔT > ΔT_crit)
- High noise + near bifurcation: Frequent flipping between states (bistable chaos)
- Compare: Run the same ΔT multiple times with noise enabled; some runs collapse, others don't (stochastic tipping risk)

**Key insight**: The real climate system, with weather and natural variability, is more fragile than deterministic models predict.

## Paleoclimate Oscillations: Dansgaard-Oeschger (D-O) Events

**Dansgaard-Oeschger events** are the most prominent rapid climate oscillations recorded during the last ice age (Marine Isotope Stage 3, ~60–25 ka):

- **Duration**: ~200–2,000 years (typically ~1,500 years)
- **Temperature jump**: ~10–15°C warming over Greenland in a few decades
- **Characteristic profile**: Rapid warming, plateau or slow cooling, then gradual return to cool baseline
- **Frequency**: Roughly every 1,500 years (not perfectly periodic)
- **Spatial pattern**: Warming over North Atlantic, anti-correlated with Antarctic cold

### AMOC Explanation (Bistability + Stochasticity)

In the context of the original Stommel model, D-O events can be understood as **stochastic oscillations in the bistable region**:

1. **Glacial background state**: ΔT is reduced compared to present (polar cold due to ice sheets), but may still be above ΔT_crit → bistable region
2. **Spontaneous warm event**: Stochastic noise (weather, ocean eddies, salinity variability) kicks AMOC from lower (collapsed) branch to upper (strong) branch
   - → AMOC suddenly strengthens → Northward heat transport increases → Greenland warms by ~10°C
3. **Warm plateau**: AMOC sustains strong state for 200–2,000 years due to positive thermal feedback
4. **Gradual cooling phase**: Slow salinity drift (via relaxation toward equilibrium, or ice sheet surges) weakens AMOC
5. **Abrupt collapse**: Another stochastic fluctuation or slow parameter change triggers collapse back to lower branch
   - → AMOC suddenly weakens → Heat transport drops → Greenland cools
6. **Return to baseline**: System sits on collapsed lower branch for ~1,500 years until next stochastic kick

### Key Insights

- **D-O events are NOT hysteresis** in the classical sense (no external parameter oscillation)
- **D-O events ARE stochastic transitions** in a bistable system
- **The 1,500-year periodicity** may reflect ocean relaxation timescale (~1,000 years) + stochastic forcing statistics
- **Model predictions align with data**: Stommel-type models do produce D-O-like oscillations when noise and paleoclimate boundary conditions (reduced ΔT) are included

## Critical Slowing Down: An Early-Warning Signal

As ΔT approaches ΔT_crit, the system's response to perturbations slows dramatically. This **critical slowing down** is the hallmark of approaching a bifurcation.

### Mechanism

The decay rate of perturbations is governed by the eigenvalue:

$$\mu_1 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^* \to 0^- \text{ as } \Delta T \to \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$$

The recovery timescale is:

$$\tau = -\frac{1}{\mu_1} \to \infty \text{ as } \Delta T \to \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$$

### Manifestations of Critical Slowing Down

1. **Increased autocorrelation**: Time series becomes more "red" (power concentrated at low frequencies)
   - Autocorrelation function decays more slowly
   - Can be quantified by lag-1 autocorrelation: $\rho_1 \to 1$ near bifurcation

2. **Increased variance**: System fluctuations amplify in response to noise
   - Variance $\sim \sqrt{-1/\mu_1} \to \infty$
   - Standard deviation of circulation or salinity rises

3. **Spectral reddening**: Frequency content shifts toward lower frequencies
   - Power spectrum peaks at frequencies near $\mu_1$ (very low frequency near bifurcation)
   - Harder to distinguish signal from noise

### Observable Precursors in Climate Data

In paleoclimate records or modern observations, we can potentially detect:
- Rising autocorrelation of AMOC strength (if measured via proxies or models)
- Increasing variability in Atlantic surface salinity or temperature
- Spectral shifts toward interdecadal timescales
- Increased occurrence of extreme events in circulation

These are **early-warning signals** or **canaries in the coal mine** that the system is approaching ΔT_crit.

### Detection Challenges

- **Noise obscures signals**: Real climate has weather noise, making weak trends hard to detect
- **Limited observational record**: AMOC observations (RAPID array) only ~20 years; paleoclimate resolution is coarse
- **False alarms**: Other processes (wind-driven variability, coupled atmosphere-ocean oscillations) can mimic critical slowing down signatures

## Can We Detect AMOC Approaching the Critical ΔT?

The key question is: **Is AMOC currently approaching ΔT_crit, or is there still a large safety margin?**

### Key Observations and Estimates

**Temperature gradient (the critical parameter)**:
- **Present-day ΔT**: ~20°C (equator ~25°C, high latitude ~5°C)
- **Estimated ΔT_crit**: ~10–15°C (from Stommel's original parameters)
- **Safety margin**: ~5–10°C above critical threshold

However:
- **Polar amplification**: Arctic warming 2–3× faster than global average → ΔT is *decreasing*
- **Equatorial convection**: Tropical warming is also significant → net ΔT change is complex
- **Projections by 2100**: Some models suggest ΔT could decrease by 2–4°C toward the critical range under high-warming scenarios

### Recent Evidence on AMOC Weakening

- **Boers (2021)**: Atlantic sea surface temperature patterns show indicators of critical slowing down. *Statistical significance debated* by community.
- **RAPID-MOCHA direct measurements** (2004–present): Show decadal variability; no definitive long-term trend yet (record too short).
- **Paleoclimate consensus** (Caesar et al. 2018, Ditlevsen & Ditlevsen 2023): AMOC has weakened ~10–15% over the past ~150 years (since 1900), but mechanism is debated.

### The ΔT-Based Interpretation

From the Stommel model perspective:
- AMOC is **likely in the bistable region** today (ΔT > ΔT_crit)
- AMOC is **likely weakening** due to various causes (freshwater, heat redistribution)
- **Tipping risk timeline**:
  - Very unlikely this century if ΔT remains >15°C
  - Moderate risk by 2100 if ΔT decreases to 12–15°C (low-confidence scenarios)
  - Higher risk if polar regions cool faster (e.g., ice sheet dynamics, heat redistribution)

### Paleoclimate Perspective

During glacial periods (Last Glacial Maximum):
- ΔT was smaller (~15–18°C, with polar ice cooling high latitudes relative to today)
- AMOC was likely in a bistable or collapsed state
- D-O events suggest frequent AMOC toggling, consistent with reduced ΔT and high stochastic tipping risk

This underscores the importance of understanding **how climate change modifies ΔT**, not just how much CO₂ increases or freshwater flux changes.
