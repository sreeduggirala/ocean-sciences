# Linear Stability Analysis

## The Principle: Linearization

To determine whether an equilibrium is stable or unstable, we linearize the ODEs around the fixed point and compute eigenvalues of the **Jacobian matrix**.

Given the original Stommel (1961) system with **relaxation forcing**:

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1)$$

$$\frac{dS_2}{dt} = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2)$$

where $q = q(S_1, S_2) = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta(S_1 - S_2)]$ and $\Delta T = T_1 - T_2$ is held fixed.

At an equilibrium $(S_1^*, S_2^*)$, let $\delta S_1 = S_1 - S_1^*$ and $\delta S_2 = S_2 - S_2^*$ be small perturbations.

Expanding the time derivatives to first order in $\delta S$:

$$\frac{d(\delta S_1)}{dt} = \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial S_1}\bigg|_* \delta S_1 + \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial S_2}\bigg|_* \delta S_2$$

$$\frac{d(\delta S_2)}{dt} = \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial S_1}\bigg|_* \delta S_1 + \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial S_2}\bigg|_* \delta S_2$$

where $f_1 = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1)$ and $f_2 = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2)$.

In compact form:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{\delta}}{dt} = J \mathbf{\delta}$$

where $\mathbf{\delta} = (\delta S_1, \delta S_2)^T$ and $J$ is the Jacobian matrix.

---

## Computing the Jacobian

### Partial Derivatives of q

First, compute the derivatives of $q(S_1, S_2) = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta(S_1 - S_2)]$:

$$\frac{\partial q}{\partial S_1} = -k\beta$$

$$\frac{\partial q}{\partial S_2} = +k\beta$$

Note: $\Delta T$ is held fixed during linearization, so it doesn't contribute to these partials.

### Jacobian Elements

For row 1: $f_1 = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1)$

$$J_{11} = \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial S_1} = -\lambda - \frac{\partial q}{\partial S_1}(S_2 - S_1) - q = -\lambda + k\beta(S_2 - S_1) - q$$

$$= -\lambda - k\beta(S_1 - S_2) - q = -\lambda - k\beta \Delta S - q$$

$$J_{12} = \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial S_2} = \frac{\partial q}{\partial S_2}(S_2 - S_1) + q = -k\beta(S_2 - S_1) + q$$

$$= k\beta(S_1 - S_2) + q = k\beta \Delta S + q$$

For row 2: $f_2 = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2)$

$$J_{21} = \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial S_1} = \frac{\partial q}{\partial S_1}(S_1 - S_2) + q = -k\beta(S_1 - S_2) + q$$

$$= -k\beta \Delta S + q$$

$$J_{22} = \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial S_2} = -\lambda - \frac{\partial q}{\partial S_2}(S_1 - S_2) - q = -\lambda - k\beta(S_1 - S_2) - q$$

$$= -\lambda - k\beta \Delta S - q$$

So the Jacobian is:

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} -\lambda - k\beta \Delta S - q & k\beta \Delta S + q \\ -k\beta \Delta S + q & -\lambda - k\beta \Delta S - q \end{pmatrix}$$

---

## Eigenvalues and the Stability-Determining Condition

Working with the new coordinate $\Delta S = S_1 - S_2$ and defining the mean $\overline{S} = (S_1 + S_2)/2$, we can decouple the system.

The key dynamics are governed by $\Delta S$ evolution:

$$\frac{d(\Delta S)}{dt} = -2\lambda(\Delta S - \Delta S^0) + 2q(\Delta S)$$

At equilibrium ($d(\Delta S)/dt = 0$), we have the condition we derived earlier in steady-state analysis. To determine stability, we linearize around $(\Delta S)^* $:

$$\frac{d[\delta(\Delta S)]}{dt} = \left[-2\lambda + 2\frac{\partial q}{\partial(\Delta S)}\right]\bigg|_* \delta(\Delta S)$$

Since $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$:

$$\frac{\partial q}{\partial(\Delta S)} = -k\beta$$

So the stability-determining eigenvalue is:

$$\mu_1 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^*$$

**Physical interpretation**:
- $-2\lambda$ term: Relaxation provides damping (always stabilizing)
- $-2k\beta(\Delta S)^*$ term: Salinity's effect on circulation (negative for large ΔS, destabilizing)

For the upper branch with large positive $(\Delta S)^*$:
$$\mu_1 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^* < 0$$

The eigenvalue is **always negative** → **stable** ✅

**Key difference from constant-F model**: The relaxation term $-2\lambda$ always contributes to stability, making the upper branch robust.

The second eigenvalue corresponds to the mean salinity evolution (affected by the total relaxation) and also has $-\lambda$ as its decay rate.

---

## Physical Interpretation of Eigenvalues

### Relaxation-Driven Stability ($-2\lambda$)

In the original Stommel model with relaxation, the equilibrium is always pulled back toward the climate target salinities $S_1^0$ and $S_2^0$ via the $-\lambda$ term. This provides a **persistent restoring force** that stabilizes the system against perturbations.

### Circulation-Driven Instability ($-2k\beta \Delta S$)

When the salinity difference is large, the circulation-driven feedback becomes significant. For large positive $\Delta S$ (upper branch):
- Large q drives salt northward, maintaining large $\Delta S$
- But this is counteracted by the relaxation force
- Net result: **stable** (relaxation wins)

### The Bifurcation Condition

The system transitions from bistable (two stable equilibria) to monostable (one state) when the upper and lower branches annihilate. This occurs when:

$$\Delta T = \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$$

At this point, the eigenvalue $\mu_1$ of one branch touches zero, indicating a **saddle-node bifurcation**.

**Key difference from constant-F model**: The bifurcation is controlled by **temperature** (an external climate parameter), not by **freshwater flux** (which can be varied externally). This makes the original model more relevant to paleoclimate transitions.

---

## Stability of the Three Branches

In the **original Stommel model with relaxation**, we evaluate:

$$\mu_1 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^*$$

### Upper Branch (Strong Thermally-Driven AMOC)

**Typical values** (Stommel's original parameters, present-day):
- $q^* \approx 5-10 \times 10^{-7}$ (1/s)  [~15–20 Sv]
- $(\Delta S)^* \approx +10$ psu
- $\lambda = 3 \times 10^{-11}$ s⁻¹

**Eigenvalue**:
$$\mu_1 = -2 \times 3 \times 10^{-11} - 2 \times 3 \times 10^{-9} \times 10$$

$$= -6 \times 10^{-11} - 6 \times 10^{-8}$$

$$\approx -6 \times 10^{-8} < 0$$ → **STABLE** ✅

### Lower Branch (Weak/Haline-Dominated Collapsed State)

**Typical values** (same parameters, but in collapsed regime):
- $q^* \approx 0$ or negative (1/s)
- $(\Delta S)^* \approx -2$ to +2 psu
- $\lambda = 3 \times 10^{-11}$ s⁻¹

**Eigenvalue**:
$$\mu_1 = -2 \times 3 \times 10^{-11} - 2 \times 3 \times 10^{-9} \times 2$$

$$= -6 \times 10^{-11} - 1.2 \times 10^{-8}$$

$$\approx -1.2 \times 10^{-8} < 0$$ → **STABLE** ✅

**Key insight**: Both branches are stable when $\lambda$ is present! Relaxation dominates and keeps both the upper and lower states stable. This is the essence of **bistability**.

### Middle Branch (Saddle-Node)

At the saddle-node bifurcation ($\Delta T = \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$):
$$\mu_1 = 0$$

This is the critical condition separating the two branches. It occurs exactly when the discriminant of the equilibrium quadratic equals zero.

---

## Bifurcation Condition

At a saddle-node bifurcation, the eigenvalue $\mu_1 = 0$:

$$0 = -2\lambda - 2k\beta(\Delta S)^*$$

$$k\beta(\Delta S)^* = -\lambda$$

$$(\Delta S)^* = -\frac{\lambda}{k\beta}$$

But in the equilibrium condition, we also require that the discriminant of the quadratic equals zero:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2k\alpha\Delta T_{\text{crit}} + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0 = 0$$

Solving for $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$:

$$2k\alpha\Delta T_{\text{crit}} + \lambda = \sqrt{8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0}$$

$$\Delta T_{\text{crit}} = \frac{\sqrt{8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0} - \lambda}{2k\alpha}$$

**Key point**: The bifurcation is determined by **temperature difference** $\Delta T$, not by freshwater forcing. When $\Delta T < \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$, the system loses the bistable regime and collapses.

In bifurcation diagrams (q vs. ΔT), we typically show:

- **Solid lines**: Stable equilibria ($\mu_1 < 0$)
- **Dashed lines**: Unstable equilibria ($\mu_1 > 0$) — rarely observed
- **Red diamonds**: Bifurcation points ($\mu_1 = 0$)

## Basins of Attraction

In the bistable region ($\Delta T > \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$), two stable equilibria coexist. The **saddle-node (unstable middle branch) acts as a separatrix**:

- Initial conditions with **larger ΔS** (more salty equator) evolve toward the **upper stable branch** (strong AMOC).
- Initial conditions with **smaller ΔS** (fresher equator) evolve toward the **lower stable branch** (weak/collapsed AMOC).
- The separatrix is a 1D unstable manifold in the 2D phase space.

This explains the **phase portraits** shown in the simulator:
- Red stars mark initial conditions (S₁₀, S₂₀)
- Colored trajectories show evolution from initial to final state
- Converging to upper or lower branch depends on the initial salinity difference

---

## Practical Implications

### 1. Sensitive Dependence on Initial Conditions

In the bistable region, two very similar initial conditions (differing by ~0.5 psu in salinity difference) can converge to *different* stable states. This is **not chaos** (trajectories are deterministic and smooth) but illustrates the **memory effect**: which attractor you reach depends on paleoclimate history.

### 2. Noise-Induced Transitions

Small random perturbations (noise) can gradually shift a trajectory across the separatrix over centuries, causing a transition from one stable state to the other. This is **noise-induced tipping** — even without changing ΔT, stochastic climate variability alone can flip the system. This is especially relevant for paleoclimate (e.g., Dansgaard-Oeschger events).

### 3. Sharp Collapse Below $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$

Once ΔT drops below $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$ (e.g., during cooling or polar amplification reversal), the upper stable branch **annihilates**. The system must collapse to the lower branch. This is a **forced tipping** — not noise-induced, but inevitable. At that instant, the collapse can happen rapidly (decades to centuries depending on relaxation timescale).

## Extensions and Generalizations

### Relaxation Timescale Effects

The relaxation rate λ acts as a **damping mechanism**. Slower relaxation ($\lambda \to 0$) makes the upper branch less stable and lowers $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$ (easier to collapse). Faster relaxation ($\lambda \to \infty$) makes both branches very stable. Real oceans are thought to have $\lambda \approx 3 \times 10^{-11}$ s⁻¹ (~1000 years), a compromise between these extremes.

### Multi-Box Models

In 3–4 box models (more realistic geometry), the Jacobian is larger (N × N for N boxes), but the principle is the same: compute eigenvalues to classify equilibria. Bifurcations typically occur when one eigenvalue crosses zero. More boxes allow for more complex phenomena (multiple tipping points, oscillations) but lose analytical tractability.

### Time-Delay Effects

If we include memory in the circulation response (e.g., $q(t)$ depends on salinity at time $t - \tau$), the Jacobian becomes an infinite-dimensional operator, and new phenomena like oscillations and chaos can arise. This is beyond the two-box model but relevant for ocean dynamics with finite adjustment timescales.

### Stochastic Stability (Noise-Induced Tipping)

When we add noise to the ODEs, we should use **stochastic bifurcation theory**, which differs subtly from deterministic stability analysis. Key insight:
- **Noise can shift bifurcation thresholds**: A system can tip before reaching the deterministic bifurcation point $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$.
- **Stochastic resonance**: The interplay between noise and the relaxation timescale can amplify certain frequencies of temperature variability.

This is why the simulator includes a noise slider — to explore how unresolved climate variability affects tipping risk.
