# Steady States and Equilibrium Analysis

## The Equilibrium Condition

At steady state, the time derivatives vanish:

$$\frac{dS_e}{dt} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{dS_p}{dt} = 0$$

This requires:

$$F = q \Delta S$$

where $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$ and $\Delta S = S_e - S_p$.

Substituting:

$$F = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot \Delta S$$

$$F = k \alpha \Delta T \cdot \Delta S - k \beta (\Delta S)^2$$

Rearranging into standard quadratic form:

$$k\beta (\Delta S)^2 - k \alpha \Delta T \cdot \Delta S + F = 0$$

---

## The Quadratic Formula

Using the quadratic formula with $a = k\beta$, $b = -k\alpha\Delta T$, $c = F$:

$$\Delta S = \frac{k\alpha\Delta T \pm \sqrt{(k\alpha\Delta T)^2 - 4k\beta F}}{2k\beta}$$

The two solutions are:

$$\Delta S_+ = \frac{k\alpha\Delta T + \sqrt{\Delta_{\text{disc}}}}{2k\beta} \quad \text{(upper branch, stronger AMOC)}$$

$$\Delta S_- = \frac{k\alpha\Delta T - \sqrt{\Delta_{\text{disc}}}}{2k\beta} \quad \text{(lower branch, weaker AMOC)}$$

Where the **discriminant** is:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (k\alpha\Delta T)^2 - 4k\beta F$$

---

## Three Regimes

### 1. Bistable Region: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} > 0$

**Condition**: $F < F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta}$

**Result**: Two real, distinct roots → **two stable equilibria**

- **Upper branch** ($\Delta S_+$): Larger $\Delta S$, larger positive $q$ → strong AMOC state
  - Higher equatorial salinity $S_e^*$, lower polar salinity $S_p^*$
  - Circulation is northward and vigorous
  - This is the **attractor** (stable)

- **Lower branch** ($\Delta S_-$): Smaller $\Delta S$, smaller (possibly negative) $q$ → weak/reversed state
  - Lower equatorial salinity, higher polar salinity
  - Circulation is weak or reversed
  - Can be either stable or unstable depending on exact parameters

- **Unstable middle branch**: Where $\lambda_1 = 0$ (see [Stability Analysis](/theory/stability-analysis))
  - Acts as a **saddle point** separating the two basins of attraction
  - Invisible in long-term simulations but critical for understanding tipping

---

### 2. Critical Point: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} = 0$

**Condition**: $F = F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta}$

**Result**: One repeated root → **saddle-node bifurcation**

$$\Delta S^* = \frac{k\alpha\Delta T}{2k\beta} = \frac{\alpha\Delta T}{2\beta}$$

This is the **tipping point**. At this point:
- The upper and lower branches "collide" and annihilate.
- Stability of the equilibrium transitions from stable to unstable (or vice versa).
- The system can no longer be in a stable state if forced beyond $F_{\text{crit}}$.

---

### 3. Monostable Region: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} < 0$

**Condition**: $F > F_{\text{crit}}$

**Result**: No real solutions... *theoretically*

But wait — how can there be no equilibrium? Physically, the system must settle *somewhere*. The resolution is that in this region, the equilibrium is **unstable in the salinity equation alone** but is saved by the interaction with freshwater and temperature fields. More carefully:

- In the true two-box model, as F increases beyond $F_{\text{crit}}$, the system enters a regime where the haline feedback dominates so strongly that even the freshwater forcing cannot sustain a stable equilibrium in the original salinity-difference coordinate.
- The system is either **continuously being pushed** toward a new state (not quasi-equilibrium) or enters a **time-dependent oscillatory regime**.

In practice, integrating the ODEs beyond $F_{\text{crit}}$ often leads to a **collapsed state** with very weak circulation or even reversal.

---

## Critical Freshwater Flux: Formula and Interpretation

$$F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta}$$

### Physical Interpretation

- **Numerator**: $k(\alpha\Delta T)^2$ measures the *strength* of thermal forcing (large ΔT and large k → strong thermal drive).
- **Denominator**: $4\beta$ measures the *strength* of salinity's effect on density (large β → salinity variations strongly affect density).

**Corollary**:
- **Large ΔT** → easier to sustain AMOC → higher $F_{\text{crit}}$ → can tolerate more freshwater before tipping.
- **Large β** → salinity effect is strong → lower $F_{\text{crit}}$ → collapse happens at lower freshwater input.

### Example with Default Parameters

- α = 1.5×10⁻⁴ /°C
- β = 8×10⁻⁴ /psu
- ΔT = 20°C (T_e = 25°C, T_p = 5°C)
- k = 1.5×10⁻⁶ 1/s

$$F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \times (1.5 \times 10^{-4} \times 20)^2}{4 \times 8 \times 10^{-4}}$$

$$= \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \times (3 \times 10^{-3})^2}{3.2 \times 10^{-3}}$$

$$= \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \times 9 \times 10^{-6}}{3.2 \times 10^{-3}}$$

$$\approx 4.2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ psu/s}$$

But the **hysteresis window** (where both states are stable) extends from a *lower* critical value $F_{c2}$ (recovery threshold) to *upper* value $F_{c1}$ (collapse threshold), with $F_{c2} < F_{c1} < F_{\text{crit}}$.

---

## The S-Curve (Bifurcation Diagram)

Plotting $q$ vs. $F$ for varying freshwater forcing reveals the characteristic **S-shaped** (fold) bifurcation diagram:

```
  q (circulation strength in Sv)
  ^
  |     _____
  |    /
  |   /
  |  |
  |   \___
  |       \____
  +------+------+---> F (freshwater flux)
       F_c2  F_c1  F_crit
```

- **Upper branch** (solid blue): Thermally-driven states, q ≈ 15 Sv initially
  - Stable as long as F < F_c1 (forward tipping point)
  - Slope: dq/dF < 0 (increasing F weakens circulation)

- **Lower branch** (solid orange): Weak/haline-driven states, q ≈ 0 or negative
  - Stable for F < F_c2 (backward recovery threshold)
  - Slope: dq/dF can be positive or negative

- **Middle branch** (dashed grey): Unstable/saddle states
  - Never observed in long simulations
  - Connects the upper and lower branches at bifurcation points
  - Represents the "forbidden zone" for stable equilibria

- **Orange shaded region**: Bistable region ($F_{c2} < F < F_{c1}$)
  - System can be in *either* state depending on initial conditions
  - Hysteresis is the defining feature
  - The "memory" of initial conditions persists even as F changes

## Computing Roots Numerically

In code, we find the roots of the quadratic:

```python
import numpy as np

def q_equilibria(F, alpha, beta, k, Delta_T):
    """Find the two equilibrium q values for given F."""
    a = k * beta
    b = -k * alpha * Delta_T
    c = F

    # Quadratic formula
    discriminant = b**2 - 4*a*c

    if discriminant < 0:
        return None, None  # No real roots
    elif discriminant == 0:
        return -b / (2*a), None  # One repeated root (bifurcation)
    else:
        sqrt_disc = np.sqrt(discriminant)
        Delta_S_plus = (-b + sqrt_disc) / (2*a)
        Delta_S_minus = (-b - sqrt_disc) / (2*a)

        # Convert back to q
        q_plus = k * (alpha * Delta_T - beta * Delta_S_plus)
        q_minus = k * (alpha * Delta_T - beta * Delta_S_minus)

        return q_plus, q_minus
```

## Physical Meaning of the Two Branches

### Upper Branch (Strong AMOC)

- **Circulation** is thermally driven: $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] > 0$
- **Large positive ΔS**: Equatorial box is much saltier than polar box
- **Energy balance**: Warm equatorial water carries salt northward → polar box becomes salty and dense → strong sinking
- **Feedback**: Stronger circulation → more salt transport north → even stronger density gradient → **self-reinforcing** (stable)
- **Observed analogue**: Present-day AMOC with ~17 Sv of transport

### Lower Branch (Weak/Reversed AMOC)

- **Circulation** is weak or haline-driven: $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \lesssim 0$
- **Small or negative ΔS**: Polar box is fresh relative to equatorial box
- **Energy balance**: Freshwater at pole blocks sinking → weak northward circulation or reversal
- **Feedback**: Weaker circulation → less salt transport north → polar box stays fresh → **self-reinforcing** (can be stable)
- **Observed analogue**: Collapsed AMOC states during Ice Age (inferred from paleoclimate proxies)

---

## Transition Behavior: Forward and Backward Paths

When we slowly increase F (as might happen with increasing freshwater input from glacial melt):

**Forward path (increasing F)**:
1. Start at F = 0 on the upper branch: q ≈ q_max, strong AMOC
2. Increase F gradually
3. Upper branch equilibrium q decreases (circulation weakens)
4. At F = F_c1 (forward tipping point), the upper branch terminates
5. **System jumps** to the lower branch → q collapses suddenly
6. Further increase in F: continue along lower branch with weak q

**Backward path (decreasing F from high value)**:
1. Start at F = high on the lower branch: q ≈ q_weak, circulation very weak
2. Decrease F gradually
3. Lower branch equilibrium remains weak
4. Lower branch does *not* rejoin upper branch immediately
5. Continue decreasing until F = F_c2 (recovery threshold), which is *lower* than F_c1
6. At F_c2, lower branch terminates and system jumps back to upper branch
7. Further decrease: continue on upper branch with strong q

**The key**: F_c2 < F_c1 → **hysteresis window** of width ΔF = F_c1 − F_c2

## Stability Classification Revisited

At each equilibrium, the Jacobian eigenvalue (see [Stability Analysis](/theory/stability-analysis)) determines stability:

$$\lambda_1 = -2q + 2k\beta \Delta S$$

**Upper branch** (large q, large ΔS):
- $-2q$ term is large and negative
- $+2k\beta \Delta S$ term is positive but smaller
- Net: $\lambda_1 < 0$ → **stable** ✅

**Lower branch** (small q, small ΔS):
- $-2q$ term is small or zero
- $+2k\beta \Delta S$ term is small or zero
- Stability depends on the specific regime; can be stable or unstable

**Middle branch** (unstable):
- Exactly where $\lambda_1 = 0$
- Transition from stable (upper) to unstable (lower) or vice versa

---

## Why Equilibria Exist (Even Beyond $F_{\text{crit}}$)

A subtle point: we derived that for $F > F_{\text{crit}}$, the quadratic has no real roots. Yet the physical system must go *somewhere*. The resolution:

1. **The two-box model is a reduced system**: It assumes salinity is the only state variable. In reality, temperature can adjust, spatial structure matters, and mixing processes complicate things.

2. **Beyond $F_{\text{crit}}$, circulation may oscillate**: Rather than a fixed equilibrium, the system may exhibit time-dependent behavior (limit cycles or chaos). This is beyond the scope of steady-state analysis.

3. **Collapsed states are quasi-stationary**: With very high F, the system sits in a quasi-steady state with very low q, high polar salinity, and slow drift. It's not a true equilibrium but a slow evolution.

In the simulator, when F exceeds $F_{\text{crit}}$ by a lot, the computed q becomes very small or negative, and the trajectory asymptotically approaches a quasi-equilibrium near the lower branch.

## Geometric Picture: The S-Curve in Parameter Space

The bifurcation diagram is really a **slice** through a higher-dimensional parameter space:

- If we vary **F** while holding α, β, k, ΔT fixed → we see the S-curve
- If we vary **ΔT** (e.g., changing polar temperature) → the S-curve shifts and changes shape
- If we vary **β** (haline contraction) → critical F and hysteresis window shift

The simulator lets you explore this space interactively:
- Adjust T_e, T_p, α, β, k in the UI
- Recompute bifurcation diagram
- Watch how the S-curve deforms and moves
