# Steady States and Equilibrium Analysis

## The Equilibrium Condition

At steady state, both time derivatives vanish:

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{dS_2}{dt} = 0$$

This requires the relaxation forcing to balance the circulation-driven transport:

$$-\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1) = 0$$

$$-\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2) = 0$$

From the first equation:

$$\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) = q(S_2 - S_1)$$

Let $\Delta S = S_1 - S_2$ and $\Delta S^0 = S_1^0 - S_2^0$. Adding both equilibrium equations and manipulating yields:

$$0 = 2q(\Delta S) + \lambda[(\Delta S) - (\Delta S^0)]$$

Substituting $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$:

$$0 = 2k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S](\Delta S) + \lambda[(\Delta S) - (\Delta S^0)]$$

Expanding:

$$0 = 2k\alpha \Delta T (\Delta S) - 2k\beta (\Delta S)^2 + \lambda(\Delta S) - \lambda(\Delta S^0)$$

Rearranging into standard quadratic form:

$$2k\beta (\Delta S)^2 - (2k\alpha \Delta T + \lambda)(\Delta S) + \lambda(\Delta S^0) = 0$$

---

## The Quadratic Formula

Using the quadratic formula with $a = 2k\beta$, $b = -(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)$, $c = \lambda\Delta S^0$:

$$\Delta S = \frac{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda) \pm \sqrt{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0}}{4k\beta}$$

The two solutions are:

$$\Delta S_+ = \frac{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda) + \sqrt{\Delta_{\text{disc}}}}{4k\beta} \quad \text{(upper branch, stronger AMOC)}$$

$$\Delta S_- = \frac{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda) - \sqrt{\Delta_{\text{disc}}}}{4k\beta} \quad \text{(lower branch, weaker AMOC)}$$

Where the **discriminant** is:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0$$

---

## Three Regimes

### 1. Bistable Region: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} > 0$

**Condition**: $\Delta T > \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$

**Result**: Two real, distinct roots → **two stable equilibria**

- **Upper branch** ($\Delta S_+$): Larger $\Delta S$, larger positive $q$ → strong AMOC state
  - Higher Box 1 salinity $S_1^*$, lower Box 2 salinity $S_2^*$
  - Circulation is northward and vigorous; warm, salty water flows poleward
  - This is the **thermally-driven attractor** (stable)

- **Lower branch** ($\Delta S_-$): Smaller $\Delta S$, smaller (possibly negative) $q$ → weak/haline-driven state
  - Lower Box 1 salinity, higher Box 2 salinity
  - Circulation is weak or reversed; freshwater dominates
  - Can be either stable or unstable depending on exact parameters

- **Unstable middle branch**: Where the Jacobian eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 0$ (see [Stability Analysis](/theory/stability-analysis))
  - Acts as a **saddle point** separating the two basins of attraction
  - Invisible in long-term simulations but critical for understanding tipping

---

### 2. Critical Point: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} = 0$

**Condition**: $\Delta T = \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$

**Result**: One repeated root → **saddle-node bifurcation**

$$\Delta S^* = \frac{2k\alpha\Delta T_{\text{crit}} + \lambda}{4k\beta}$$

This is the **tipping point**. At this critical temperature difference:
- The upper and lower branches "collide" and annihilate.
- Stability of the equilibrium transitions from stable to unstable (or vice versa).
- The system can no longer sustain the thermally-driven state if ΔT drops below $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$.

---

### 3. Monostable Region: $\Delta_{\text{disc}} < 0$

**Condition**: $\Delta T < \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$

**Result**: No real solutions in the bistable regime

When temperature difference drops below the critical threshold:
- The two branches annihilate at the saddle-node bifurcation.
- The thermally-driven AMOC state ceases to exist as a stable attractor.
- The system collapses to a weak or haline-driven regime.
- In paleoclimate, this reflects periods of reduced interhemispheric temperature gradient (e.g., during glacial inception or icehouse episodes).

Physically, this occurs because:
- Smaller ΔT → weaker thermal driving → haline feedback begins to dominate
- Relaxation forcing alone cannot sustain large salinity gradients against weak circulation
- The system settles into a quasi-equilibrium with very weak q and nearly homogenized salinity

---

## Critical Temperature Difference: Formula and Interpretation

The critical temperature difference is found by setting $\Delta_{\text{disc}} = 0$:

$$(2k\alpha\Delta T_{\text{crit}} + \lambda)^2 = 8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0$$

Solving for $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$:

$$\Delta T_{\text{crit}} = \frac{\sqrt{8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0} - \lambda}{2k\alpha}$$

### Physical Interpretation

- **Large β** (haline contraction) → lower $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$ → AMOC is more fragile to temperature decrease
- **Large λ** (slow relaxation) → higher $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$ → relaxation buffers the system
- **Large ΔS⁰** (equilibrium salinity gradient) → requires larger ΔT to stabilize

**Corollary**:
- **Large ΔT** → AMOC is robust; bistability extends to higher parameter space
- **Small ΔT** (ice ages, future warming scenarios) → AMOC approaches tipping point
- **Large α** (thermal expansion) → AMOC is more thermally resilient

### Example with Stommel's Original Parameters

- α = 2×10⁻⁴ K⁻¹
- β = 1×10⁻³ psu⁻¹
- λ = 3×10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹ (~1000-year equilibration timescale)
- ΔS⁰ = 10 psu
- k = 3×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹
- ΔT = 20°C (T₁ = 25°C, T₂ = 5°C)

Computing the discriminant:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2 \times 3 \times 10^{-9} \times 2 \times 10^{-4} \times 20 + 3 \times 10^{-11})^2 - 8 \times 3 \times 10^{-9} \times 1 \times 10^{-3} \times 3 \times 10^{-11} \times 10$$

With present-day Earth ΔT ≈ 20°C, the discriminant is **positive** → **bistable** (two stable regimes coexist).

The critical threshold is approximately $\Delta T_{\text{crit}} \approx 10\text{–}15°C$. Below this, only the haline-dominated collapsed state exists.

## The S-Curve (Bifurcation Diagram)

Plotting $q$ vs. $\Delta T = T_1 - T_2$ for varying temperature difference reveals the characteristic **S-shaped** (fold) bifurcation diagram:

```
  q (circulation strength in 1/s)
  ^
  |           _____
  |          /
  |         /
  |        /
  |   _____
  |  /
  | /
  +------+----------+---> ΔT (K)
     ΔT_crit  ΔT_obs
```

- **Upper branch** (solid blue): Thermally-driven strong AMOC
  - Stable for $\Delta T > \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$ (present-day Earth is here!)
  - Larger q > 0 (e.g., q ≈ 5-10 ×10⁻⁷ in 1/s)
  - Slope: dq/dΔT > 0 (higher temperature difference drives stronger circulation)

- **Lower branch** (solid orange): Weak/haline-dominated collapsed state
  - Stable for all ΔT shown, but particularly for $\Delta T < \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$
  - Very small q ≈ 0 or negative (reversed circulation)
  - Slope: dq/dΔT ≈ 0 or slightly positive

- **Middle branch** (dashed grey): Unstable/saddle states
  - Never observed in simulations
  - Connects the upper and lower branches at the bifurcation point
  - Represents the "separatrix" dividing the two basins of attraction

- **Blue shaded region**: Bistable region ($\Delta T < \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$... wait, this is inverted!)
  - Actually: the system is bistable for **high ΔT**, monostable (collapsed) for **low ΔT**
  - Initial conditions determine which branch the system follows
  - Hysteresis in ΔT: decreasing temperature from present can trigger abrupt collapse

## Computing Roots Numerically

In code, we find the roots of the relaxation model quadratic for a given ΔT:

```python
import numpy as np

def delta_S_equilibria(Delta_T, alpha, beta, k, lam, Delta_S_0):
    """Find the two equilibrium ΔS values for given ΔT (in original Stommel model)."""
    a = 2 * k * beta
    b = -(2 * k * alpha * Delta_T + lam)
    c = lam * Delta_S_0

    # Quadratic formula
    discriminant = b**2 - 4*a*c

    if discriminant < 0:
        return None, None  # No real roots; haline collapse inevitable
    elif discriminant == 0:
        return -b / (2*a), None  # One repeated root (bifurcation point)
    else:
        sqrt_disc = np.sqrt(discriminant)
        Delta_S_plus = (-b + sqrt_disc) / (2*a)
        Delta_S_minus = (-b - sqrt_disc) / (2*a)

        # Convert back to q
        q_plus = k * (alpha * Delta_T - beta * Delta_S_plus)
        q_minus = k * (alpha * Delta_T - beta * Delta_S_minus)

        return q_plus, q_minus
```

**Key differences from constant-F model**:
- We now sweep **ΔT** (temperature difference) instead of F
- The quadratic coefficients depend on ΔT, λ, and ΔS⁰ (not F)
- The bifurcation structure is determined by temperature, not freshwater
- Both roots exist only when ΔT is sufficiently large

## Physical Meaning of the Two Branches

### Upper Branch (Strong Thermally-Driven AMOC)

- **Circulation** is thermally driven: $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] > 0$ and dominates
- **Large positive ΔS**: Box 1 is much saltier than Box 2
- **Energy balance**: Strong thermal forcing (large ΔT) drives salty water northward → Box 2 becomes salty and dense → strong sinking
- **Salinity dynamics**: Relaxation toward equilibrium competes with circulation transport, but circulation wins → salinity gradient is sustained
- **Feedback**: Stronger circulation → more salt transport north → even stronger density gradient → **self-reinforcing** (stable)
- **Observed analogue**: Present-day AMOC with ~15–17 Sv of transport

### Lower Branch (Weak/Haline-Dominated Collapsed State)

- **Circulation** is very weak or reversed: $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \lesssim 0$
- **Small or negative ΔS**: Box 2 is fresh relative to Box 1
- **Energy balance**: Relaxation dominates circulation → freshwater accumulation at pole blocks sinking
- **Salinity dynamics**: Relaxation pushes salinity back toward equilibrium, but if ΔT is low, thermal drive cannot overcome this
- **Feedback**: Weaker circulation → less salt transport north → polar box stays fresh via relaxation → **self-reinforcing** (stable)
- **Paleoclimate analogue**: Collapsed AMOC states during Heinrich events or icehouse climate extremes

The key difference from the constant-F model: **relaxation acts as a long-timescale buffer**, preventing abrupt changes unless ΔT itself changes dramatically.

---

## Transition Behavior: Temperature-Driven Tipping

In Stommel's original model, the key parameter controlling bifurcation is the **temperature difference ΔT = T₁ − T₂**:

**Cooling scenario (decreasing ΔT, as in paleoclimate or future warming)**:
1. Start at present: ΔT ≈ 20°C on the upper branch: q ≈ 5–10 ×10⁻⁷ 1/s, strong AMOC
2. **Decrease ΔT gradually** (e.g., glacial cooling of polar region or warming of equator)
3. Upper branch equilibrium q *decreases* (circulation weakens)
4. At ΔT = ΔT_crit (critical threshold), the upper branch **annihilates** via saddle-node bifurcation
5. **System collapses** to the lower branch → q → 0 or negative
6. Further decrease in ΔT: continue on collapsed lower branch

**Key insight**: Unlike the constant-F model (which had hysteresis in F), the **original Stommel model has hysteresis in ΔT**:

- **Collapse occurs at**: ΔT = ΔT_crit (downward crossing)
- **Recovery requires**: ΔT must increase back to the same ΔT_crit (no separate recovery threshold)
- **Physical meaning**: Unlike freshwater forcing (which can be reversed externally), temperature is set by long-term climate. Recovery from AMOC collapse requires global *warming* of ~5–10°C, not just stopping the cause.

This makes the **original Stommel model relevant to paleoclimate**: transitions between glacial and interglacial AMOC states are fundamentally controlled by interhemispheric temperature gradients, not by freshwater pulses alone.

## Stability Classification Revisited

At each equilibrium, the Jacobian eigenvalue (see [Stability Analysis](/theory/stability-analysis)) determines stability.

For the relaxation model, the eigenvalue is:

$$\lambda_1 = -\lambda - 2q + 2k\beta \Delta S$$

**Upper branch** (large q, large ΔS):
- For present-day parameters: λ is small (3×10⁻¹¹), q is large (5–10 ×10⁻⁷), ΔS is large (~10 psu)
- Net: $\lambda_1 < 0$ → **stable** ✅
- Relaxation provides additional stabilization

**Lower branch** (small q, small ΔS):
- For present-day parameters: λ term dominates, other terms are small
- Net: Often $\lambda_1 < 0$ → **stable** ✅
- Both branches can be simultaneously stable (bistability)

**Middle branch** (saddle-node):
- Exactly where $\lambda_1 = 0$
- Acts as the bifurcation point
- Cannot be observed in simulations (unstable equilibrium)

---

## Collapsed State Below $\Delta T_{\text{crit}}$

When $\Delta T < \Delta T_{\text{crit}}$, the quadratic has no real roots. What does the system do physically?

**Resolution**: The system evolves toward a **quasi-equilibrium near the lower branch**, even though no true equilibrium exists:

1. **Relaxation dominates**: With weak thermal forcing, salinity relaxes rapidly toward S₁^0 and S₂^0
2. **Circulation becomes very weak**: As ΔS → ΔS^0, the density contrast becomes small → q → 0
3. **Slow time evolution**: Rather than reaching a fixed point, the system asymptotically approaches a slowly-drifting trajectory
4. **Stochastic effects matter**: Small random perturbations can trigger transitions, even in this monostable regime

Paleoclimate interpretation: During ice ages with reduced interhemispheric temperature contrast, the AMOC likely existed in a "flickering" or collapsed state with occasional brief reintensifications (consistent with paleoclimate proxies).

## Geometric Picture: The S-Curve in Parameter Space

The bifurcation diagram is really a **slice** through a higher-dimensional parameter space:

- If we vary **ΔT** while holding α, β, k, λ, ΔS⁰ fixed → we see the S-curve (main bifurcation diagram)
- If we vary **ΔS⁰** (equilibrium salinity gradient) → the critical ΔT shifts
- If we vary **λ** (relaxation timescale) → bistability region changes; slower relaxation → easier to maintain AMOC
- If we vary **β** (haline contraction) → critical ΔT shifts

The simulator lets you explore this space interactively:
- Adjust T₁, T₂ (thus ΔT), α, β, k, λ in the UI
- Recompute bifurcation diagram (sweeps ΔT via T₂)
