# The Two-Box Model Formulation

This is the core of Stommel's 1961 paper: a beautifully simple yet rich dynamical system that captures the essential nonlinear behavior of thermohaline circulation.

---

## Conceptual Setup

Imagine the Atlantic Ocean divided into **two well-mixed boxes**:

<div>
**Equatorial Box (E)**
- Warm: T_e = 25°C
- High salinity: S_e ≈ 36 psu
- Represents tropical Atlantic surface
- Area: A; Volume: V_box

<div>
**Polar Box (P)**
- Cold: T_p = 5°C
- Variable salinity: S_p (evolves over time)

- Same volume: V_box

## Assumptions (Critical to Remember)

The model makes several simplifying assumptions that are **critical to its tractability** but also its limitations:

1. **Temperatures are fixed** (T_e, T_p = constants)
   - Justification: Atmospheric timescale is fast (~1 year) compared to ocean salinity adjustment (~centuries). We assume the atmosphere thermostats the surface layer.
   - Reality: Temperatures do vary, but slowly on THC timescales.

2. **Salinity evolves** (S_e, S_p = state variables)
   - Justification: Salt is quasi-conservative (only removed by ice or added by evaporation); thermohaline forcing acts via freshwater, not heat.
   - Reality: Salinity is conservative over the simulation period.

3. **Circulation is instantaneous** (no inertia)
   - Justification: Acceleration timescale of the ocean (~1 day) is much shorter than salinity evolution (~10–100 years).
   - Reality: Geostrophic balance and hydrostatic equilibrium relax quickly.

4. **Boxes are well-mixed** (no spatial gradients within boxes)
   - Justification: Turbulent mixing on sub-grid scales quickly homogenizes properties within each box.
   - Reality: More complex locally, but captures large-scale averages.

5. **Linear equation of state**
   - Justification: Accurate over the small ranges (T: 5–25°C, S: 34–36 psu) relevant here.
   - Reality: Actual seawater EOS is nonlinear (especially in freshwater/cold regimes), but linear approximation is standard in oceanography.

## State Variables and Parameters

### State Variables (evolve over time)

- **S_e(t)**: Equatorial box salinity (psu)
- **S_p(t)**: Polar box salinity (psu)

### Parameters (fixed per simulation)

| Parameter | Symbol | Default | Units | Physical Meaning |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------|------------------|
| Equatorial temperature | T_e | 25 | °C | Fixed equatorial surface T |
| Polar temperature | T_p | 5 | °C | Fixed polar surface T |
| Thermal expansion | α | 1.5×10⁻⁴ | 1/°C | Density change per degree warming |
| Haline contraction | β | 8×10⁻⁴ | 1/psu | Density change per unit salinity |
| Circulation coefficient | k | 1.5×10⁻⁶ | 1/s | Empirical coupling constant |
| Freshwater flux | F | 1.0×10⁻⁴ | psu/s | Net freshwater input to polar box |
| Initial equatorial salinity | S_e0 | 36 | psu | S_e(0) |
| Initial polar salinity | S_p0 | 34 | psu | S_p(0) |

## The Core Physics: Circulation Equation

The circulation strength is driven by the **density difference** between boxes:

$$\rho_e = \rho_0[1 - \alpha T_e + \beta S_e]$$

$$\rho_p = \rho_0[1 - \alpha T_p + \beta S_p]$$

The density *difference* (equatorial minus polar):

$$\Delta \rho = \rho_e - \rho_p = \rho_0[-\alpha(T_e - T_p) + \beta(S_e - S_p)]$$

$$= \rho_0[-\alpha \Delta T + \beta \Delta S]$$

Where $\Delta T = T_e - T_p > 0$ (equator is always warmer) and $\Delta S = S_e - S_p$.

The normalized density difference (divided by ρ₀) drives circulation:

$$q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$$

### Interpretation of q

- **$q > 0$ (positive, thermally driven)**:
  - The equatorial box is effectively denser (despite being warmer, it's salty enough to outweigh thermal effect).
  - Warm, salty water flows northward from the equator; cold, fresh water returns southward at depth.
  - This is the **AMOC-like regime**: northward heat transport, carrying heat to high latitudes.

- **$q \approx 0$ (near zero, neutral)**:
  - Thermal and haline effects nearly cancel.
  - Circulation is very weak; the system is near a tipping point.

- **$q < 0$ (negative, reversed)**:
  - The polar box is effectively denser.
  - Fresh, cold water flows northward at the surface; warm, salty water returns southward at depth.
  - This is a **reversed or salinity-dominated regime**: physically possible but energetically unfavorable.

## The Dynamics: ODEs for Salinity

Salt is conserved: it is neither created nor destroyed, only advected by circulation and added/removed by freshwater fluxes.

**Salt flux terms**:

1. **Advective flux** due to circulation $q$:
   - Circulation carries salt from equatorial box to polar box at rate $-q(S_e - S_p)$ (negative because circulation removes salt from equator, adds to pole if $q > 0$).
   - But the sign is: when $q > 0$ (northward), equatorial water flows north, *removing* salt from equatorial box (rate: $-q \Delta S$) and *adding* to polar box (rate: $+q \Delta S$).

2. **Freshwater forcing** $F$:
   - $F > 0$: net freshwater added to polar box (dilutes it, so dS_p/dt gets a $-F$ term); simultaneously removed from equatorial box to conserve mass (so dS_e/dt gets a $+F$ term).
   - $F < 0$: evaporation dominates; salinity increases at pole, decreases at equator.

**The ODE system**:

$$\frac{dS_e}{dt} = -q(S_e - S_p) + F$$

$$\frac{dS_p}{dt} = +q(S_e - S_p) - F$$

### Verification: Salt Conservation

Adding the two equations:

$$\frac{d(S_e + S_p)}{dt} = -q(S_e - S_p) + F + q(S_e - S_p) - F = 0$$

Good! The total salt is conserved: $S_e(t) + S_p(t) = S_e(0) + S_p(0) = \text{const}$.

---

## Substituting the Circulation Equation

Expand $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$ in the ODEs:

$$\frac{dS_e}{dt} = -k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot (S_e - S_p) + F$$

$$= -k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot \Delta S + F$$

This is a **highly nonlinear system**: the feedback is cubic in $\Delta S$.

---

## Steady States and Equilibrium

At steady state, both time derivatives are zero:

$$0 = -q(S_e - S_p) + F = -q \Delta S + F$$

Therefore at equilibrium:

$$F = q \Delta S = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot \Delta S$$

$$F = k \alpha \Delta T \cdot \Delta S - k \beta (\Delta S)^2$$

Rearranging:

$$k \beta (\Delta S)^2 - k \alpha \Delta T \cdot \Delta S + F = 0$$

This is a **quadratic equation** in $\Delta S$. Let $a = k\beta$, $b = -k\alpha\Delta T$, $c = F$:

$$a(\Delta S)^2 + b(\Delta S) + c = 0$$

### Solutions (Quadratic Formula)

$$\Delta S = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} = \frac{k\alpha\Delta T \pm \sqrt{(k\alpha\Delta T)^2 - 4k\beta F}}{2k\beta}$$

The **discriminant** is:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (k\alpha\Delta T)^2 - 4k\beta F$$

Three regimes:

| Regime | Condition | # Solutions | Interpretation |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Bistable** | $\Delta_{\text{disc}} > 0$ | 2 real roots | Two stable equilibria exist |
| **Critical** | $\Delta_{\text{disc}} = 0$ | 1 repeated root | Saddle-node bifurcation; tipping point |
| **Monostable** | $\Delta_{\text{disc}} < 0$ | 0 real roots | No equilibrium (unstable to infinite state — physically unphysical) |

---

## Critical Freshwater Flux

The system transitions from bistable to monostable when the discriminant equals zero:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (k\alpha\Delta T)^2 - 4k\beta F_{\text{crit}} = 0$$

$$F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{(k\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4k\beta} = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta}$$

**Example with default parameters**:
- α = 1.5×10⁻⁴, ΔT = 20°C, k = 1.5×10⁻⁶, β = 8×10⁻⁴

$$F_{\text{crit}} = \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \times (1.5 \times 10^{-4} \times 20)^2}{4 \times 8 \times 10^{-4}}$$

$$= \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \times (3 \times 10^{-3})^2}{3.2 \times 10^{-3}}$$

$$\approx 4.2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ psu/s}$$

But this is the *critical* point where bistability ends. The hysteresis window (where two states coexist) is roughly $[F_1, F_2]$ with $F_2 < F_1 < F_{\text{crit}}$.

---

## Stability of Equilibria

For a given F, the quadratic yields one or two solutions for $\Delta S$. But are they stable?

Linearize around an equilibrium $(S_e^*, S_p^*)$:

$$\delta \dot{S}_e = -\frac{\partial}{\partial S_e}[-q \Delta S + F] \cdot \delta S_e - \frac{\partial}{\partial S_p}[-q \Delta S + F] \cdot \delta S_p$$

With some algebra (using $\partial q / \partial S_e = -k\beta$, $\partial q / \partial S_p = +k\beta$):

$$J_{11} = \frac{\partial \dot{S}_e}{\partial S_e} = -q^* + k\beta \Delta S^*$$

$$J_{12} = \frac{\partial \dot{S}_e}{\partial S_p} = +q^* + k\beta \Delta S^*$$

The Jacobian is:

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} -q^* + k\beta \Delta S^* & q^* + k\beta \Delta S^* \\ -(- q^* + k\beta \Delta S^*) & -(q^* + k\beta \Delta S^*) \end{pmatrix}$$

Due to the salt conservation constraint, one eigenvalue is always zero (neutral direction). The physically relevant eigenvalue is:

$$\lambda_1 = -2q^* + 2k\beta \Delta S^*$$

**Stability**:
- If $\lambda_1 < 0$: stable node (attractor)
- If $\lambda_1 > 0$: unstable node (saddle/repellor)
- If $\lambda_1 = 0$: bifurcation point

For the **strong AMOC branch** (upper branch, large q):
- $q^*$ is large and positive.
- $\Delta S^*$ is large and positive.
- Usually $\lambda_1 < 0$: **stable** (this is the attractor we see in phase space).

For the **weak/haline branch** (lower branch, small q):
- $q^*$ is small or zero.
- $\Delta S^*$ is small or negative.
- Often $\lambda_1 > 0$: **unstable** (saddle point).

The **middle branch** (unstable part of the S-curve):
- Where the two roots of the quadratic transition from stable to unstable.
- Forms the dashed line in bifurcation diagrams.

## Conversion to Sverdrups

The circulation equation gives $q$ in units of 1/s. To convert to Sverdrups (observed units):

$$q_{\text{Sv}} = q \times \frac{V_{\text{box}}}{10^6 \text{ m}^3/\text{s per Sv}}$$

With a typical Atlantic box volume of ~7×10¹⁵ m³:

$$q_{\text{Sv}} = q \times \frac{7 \times 10^{15}}{10^6} = q \times 7 \times 10^9$$

So if $q \approx 2 \times 10^{-9}$ (1/s), then $q_{\text{Sv}} \approx 14$ Sv, matching observations.

This scaling is baked into the simulator; all displayed values are in Sverdrups.

## Non-Dimensionalization (Advanced)

It's instructive to work with non-dimensional variables. Define:

- $\tilde{S} = \Delta S / (\alpha \Delta T / \beta)$: normalized salinity difference
- $\tilde{F} = F / (k \alpha \Delta T \cdot \alpha \Delta T / \beta)$: normalized freshwater flux

Then the equilibrium quadratic becomes:

$$(\tilde{\Delta S})^2 - \tilde{\Delta S} + \tilde{F} = 0$$

The only control parameter that appears is $\tilde{F}$! This shows that once you fix the thermal forcing and physical coefficients, all behavior is captured by a single non-dimensional number: the ratio of freshwater forcing to thermal forcing.

Alternatively, the **Stommel number** is sometimes defined:

$$S = \frac{\beta F}{\alpha k (\Delta T)^2}$$

For $S < S_c$ (critical value ≈ 1/4), the system is bistable.

## What the Model Captures and Misses

### ✅ Model Strengths

- **Bistability**: Two stable states exist under the same parameters.
- **Hysteresis**: Transition path depends on history.
- **Tipping points**: Saddle-node bifurcations occur at parameter values that can be computed.
- **Analytical tractability**: ODEs are simple enough to analyze by hand and numerically integrate quickly.
- **Scalability**: Captures the essential nonlinearity even when extended to 3–4 boxes or embedded in larger models.

### ❌ Model Limitations

- **No time delays**: Real ocean responds over decades; this model assumes instantaneous equilibration of circulation.
- **No wind forcing**: The AMOC actually depends on both thermohaline and wind-driven components.
- **No spatial structure**: Real THC has 3D structure, vertical mixing, and boundary layers.
- **No biology**: Nutrient cycling and oxygen dynamics are omitted.
- **No eddies**: Sub-grid turbulence is parameterized away.
- **No ice sheet dynamics**: The only ice effect is freshwater input (not ice sheet slope, extent, or rebound).

## Numerical Integration

The simulator uses a **Radau solver** (a stiff, implicit Runge-Kutta method) to integrate the ODEs from $t=0$ to $t=t_{\max}$:

$$\frac{dS_e}{dt} = -q(S_e - S_p) + F, \quad S_e(0) = S_{e0}$$

$$\frac{dS_p}{dt} = +q(S_e - S_p) - F, \quad S_p(0) = S_{p0}$$

Where $q(S_e, S_p) = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta(S_e - S_p)]$.

**With stochastic forcing** (noise_amplitude > 0), the solver switches to **Euler-Maruyama**:

$$S_e(t + dt) = S_e(t) + [-q(S_e - S_p) + F] \, dt + \sigma_F \sqrt{dt} \, \xi$$

Where $\sigma_F$ is the noise amplitude and $\xi \sim N(0, 1)$ is a Gaussian random variable.
