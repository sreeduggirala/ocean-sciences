# The Two-Box Model Formulation

This is the core of Stommel's 1961 paper: a beautifully simple yet rich dynamical system that captures the essential nonlinear behavior of thermohaline circulation. The key innovation is **relaxation-toward-equilibrium freshwater forcing**, which captures the slow adjustment of salinity to atmospheric conditions over ~1000-year timescales.

## Assumptions (Critical to Remember)

The model makes several simplifying assumptions that are **critical to its tractability** but also its limitations:

1. **Temperatures are fixed** (T₁, T₂ = constants)
   - Justification: Atmospheric timescale is fast (~1 year) compared to ocean salinity adjustment (~centuries). We assume the atmosphere thermostats the surface layer.
   - Reality: Temperatures do vary, but slowly on THC timescales.

2. **Salinity evolves** (S₁, S₂ = state variables)
   - Justification: Salt is quasi-conservative; thermohaline forcing acts via freshwater relaxation, not heat.
   - Reality: Salinity is conservative over the simulation period.

3. **Circulation is instantaneous** (no inertia)
   - Justification: Acceleration timescale of the ocean (~1 day) is much shorter than salinity evolution (~100–1000 years).
   - Reality: Geostrophic balance and hydrostatic equilibrium relax quickly.

4. **Boxes are well-mixed** (no spatial gradients within boxes)
   - Justification: Turbulent mixing on sub-grid scales quickly homogenizes properties within each box.
   - Reality: More complex locally, but captures large-scale averages.

5. **Linear equation of state**
   - Justification: Accurate over the small ranges (T: 5–25°C, S: 34–36 psu) relevant here.
   - Reality: Actual seawater EOS is nonlinear, but linear approximation is standard in oceanography.

## State Variables and Parameters

### State Variables (evolve over time)

- **S₁(t)**: Box 1 (equatorial) salinity (psu)
- **S₂(t)**: Box 2 (polar) salinity (psu)

### Parameters (fixed per simulation)

| Parameter | Symbol | Default | Units | Physical Meaning |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------|------------------|
| Box 1 temperature | T₁ | 25 | °C | Fixed equatorial surface T |
| Box 2 temperature | T₂ | 5 | °C | Fixed polar surface T |
| Box 1 equilibrium salinity | S₁⁰ | 36 | psu | Relaxation target (equator) |
| Box 2 equilibrium salinity | S₂⁰ | 34 | psu | Relaxation target (pole) |
| Thermal expansion | α | 2×10⁻⁴ | 1/°C | Density change per degree (Stommel 1961) |
| Haline contraction | β | 1×10⁻³ | 1/psu | Density change per unit salinity (Stommel 1961) |
| Circulation coefficient | k | 3×10⁻⁹ | 1/s | Empirical coupling constant (Stommel 1961) |
| Relaxation rate | λ | 3×10⁻¹¹ | 1/s | ~1000-year equilibration timescale (Stommel 1961) |

## The Core Physics: Circulation Equation

The circulation strength is driven by the **density difference** between boxes:

$$\rho_1 = \rho_0[1 - \alpha T_1 + \beta S_1]$$

$$\rho_2 = \rho_0[1 - \alpha T_2 + \beta S_2]$$

The density *difference* (Box 1 minus Box 2):

$$\Delta \rho = \rho_1 - \rho_2 = \rho_0[-\alpha(T_1 - T_2) + \beta(S_1 - S_2)]$$

$$= \rho_0[-\alpha \Delta T + \beta \Delta S]$$

where $\Delta T = T_1 - T_2 > 0$ (equator is always warmer) and $\Delta S = S_1 - S_2$ (salinity difference, usually positive).

A positive density difference drives circulation. The normalized density difference drives the circulation **strength**:

$$q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$$

### Interpretation of q

- **q > 0 (positive, thermally driven)**:
  - The equatorial box is effectively denser (despite being warmer, salinity dominance means ρ₁ > ρ₂).
  - Warm, salty water flows northward from Box 1; cold, fresh water returns southward at depth.
  - This is the **AMOC-like regime**: northward heat transport, carrying heat to high latitudes.

- **q ≈ 0 (near zero, neutral)**:
  - Thermal and haline effects nearly cancel.
  - Circulation is very weak; the system is near a tipping point.

- **q < 0 (negative, reversed)**:
  - Box 2 is effectively denser.
  - Fresh, cold water flows northward at the surface; warm, salty water returns southward at depth.
  - This is a **reversed or salinity-dominated regime**: physically possible but requires very fresh polar box.

## The Dynamics: ODEs with Relaxation Forcing

In Stommel's original model, salinity does **not** obey simple salt conservation. Instead, each box exchanges salt with the atmosphere through a **relaxation process** toward climatological equilibrium values:

$$H_i = -\lambda(S_i - S_i^0)$$

where:
- **λ ≈ 3 × 10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹** is the relaxation coefficient
- **S₁⁰, S₂⁰** are the equilibrium salinity targets (representing climatological values in the absence of meridional transport)
- **τ ≈ 1/λ ≈ 1000 years** is the equilibration timescale

**Physical interpretation**: The atmosphere preferentially adds freshwater to high latitudes (precipitation excess) and removes it from the tropics (evaporation excess). This creates persistent freshwater anomalies that equilibrate slowly (~1000 years) through ocean mixing and advection.

The **complete ODE system** is:

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) + q(S_2 - S_1)$$

$$\frac{dS_2}{dt} = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + q(S_1 - S_2)$$

### Interpretation of Terms

1. **Relaxation term** $-\lambda(S_i - S_i^0)$:
   - Negative feedback driving salinity back toward equilibrium
   - Acts on timescale τ ≈ 1000 years (slow!)

2. **Transport term** $q(S_2 - S_1)$ for Box 1 (equivalently, $q(S_1 - S_2)$ for Box 2):
   - Positive circulation (q > 0) transports salty water northward, freshening Box 1, saltening Box 2
   - Acts on timescale ~years to decades (fast!)

---

## Substituting the Circulation Equation

Expand $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$ in the ODEs:

$$\frac{dS_1}{dt} = -\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0) - k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot \Delta S$$

$$\frac{dS_2}{dt} = -\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0) + k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S] \cdot \Delta S$$

where $\Delta S = S_1 - S_2$.

This is a **highly nonlinear system**: the circulation feedback is quadratic in $\Delta S$, and the relaxation provides a slow restoring force.

---

## Steady States and Equilibrium

At steady state, both time derivatives are zero:

$$0 = -\lambda(S_1^* - S_1^0) + q^*(\Delta S)^*$$

$$0 = -\lambda(S_2^* - S_2^0) - q^*(\Delta S)^*$$

Adding these equations:

$$0 = -\lambda[(S_1^* - S_1^0) + (S_2^* - S_2^0)]$$

Define the equilibrium salinity difference as $(\Delta S)^0 = S_1^0 - S_2^0$ (typically 10 psu). If we assume **equal relaxation rates** at both boxes, the sum of the equations implies:

$$(\Delta S)^* - (\Delta S)^0 = -\frac{2q^*(\Delta S)^*}{\lambda}$$

Also, from either equation:

$$\lambda(\Delta S)^* = 2q^* (\Delta S)^* + \lambda(\Delta S)^0$$

Substituting $q^* = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta (\Delta S)^*]$:

$$0 = 2k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta (\Delta S)^*](\Delta S)^* + \lambda[(\Delta S)^* - (\Delta S)^0]$$

Expanding:

$$0 = 2k\alpha \Delta T (\Delta S)^* - 2k\beta (\Delta S)^{*2} + \lambda(\Delta S)^* - \lambda(\Delta S)^0$$

Rearranging:

$$2k\beta (\Delta S)^{*2} - (2k\alpha \Delta T + \lambda)(\Delta S)^* + \lambda(\Delta S)^0 = 0$$

This is a **quadratic equation** in $(\Delta S)^*$.

### Solutions (Quadratic Formula)

$$\Delta S = \frac{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda) \pm \sqrt{(2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda(\Delta S)^0}}{4k\beta}$$

The **discriminant** is:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda(\Delta S)^0$$

Three regimes:

| Regime | Condition | # Solutions | Interpretation |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Bistable** | Δ_disc > 0 | 2 real roots | Two stable equilibria (thermal + haline) |
| **Critical** | Δ_disc = 0 | 1 repeated root | Saddle-node bifurcation; tipping point |
| **Monostable** | Δ_disc < 0 | 0 real roots | Only one stable state |

---

## Critical Temperature Difference

The system transitions from bistable to monostable as a function of **temperature difference** ΔT:

$$\Delta_{\text{disc}} = (2k\alpha\Delta T + \lambda)^2 - 8k\beta\lambda(\Delta S)^0 = 0$$

The critical ΔT_crit is found by solving:

$$(2k\alpha\Delta T_{\text{crit}} + \lambda)^2 = 8k\beta\lambda(\Delta S)^0$$

**Example with Stommel's original parameters**:
- α = 2×10⁻⁴ K⁻¹, β = 1×10⁻³ psu⁻¹, k = 3×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹, λ = 3×10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹, (ΔS)⁰ = 10 psu

The bistable window typically spans ΔT ≈ 25–35°C, with present-day Earth at ΔT ≈ 20°C (firmly in the thermally-driven regime).

---

## Stability of Equilibria

For a given ΔT, the quadratic yields one or two solutions for $(\Delta S)^*$. But are they stable?

Linearize around an equilibrium $(\Delta S)^*$:

$$\delta \dot{S} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \Delta S}\Big|_{*} \delta(\Delta S)$$

where $f(\Delta S) = -\lambda(\Delta S - \Delta S^0) + 2k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S](\Delta S) + \ldots$

Computing the derivative:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \Delta S} = -\lambda + 2k[\alpha \Delta T - 2\beta \Delta S^*]$$

**Stability**:
- If $\frac{\partial f}{\partial S} < 0$: stable (perturbations decay)
- If $\frac{\partial f}{\partial S} > 0$: unstable (perturbations grow)

**For the thermal (strong) branch**:
- Far from bifurcation: stable
- Near bifurcation: marginally stable, growth rate → 0 (critical slowing down!)

**For the haline (weak) branch**:
- Always stable (salinity is far from its equilibrium, so relaxation dominates)

## Key Differences from Simplified Models

Stommel's **original model** (with relaxation) differs from simplified **constant-F variants**:

| Feature | Stommel 1961 (Relaxation) | Simplified (Constant F) |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Forcing** | H_i = -λ(S_i - S_i⁰) | F = constant |
| **Timescale** | ~1000 years | N/A |
| **Bifurcation control** | Temperature difference ΔT | Freshwater flux F |
| **Hysteresis** | Yes, as ΔT varies | Yes, as F varies |
| **Physical meaning** | Atmospheric relaxation | Direct freshwater input |

The relaxation model is closer to Stommel's original intent: capturing the slow adjustment of the ocean to atmospheric forcing via freshwater anomalies.
