## Detailed Parameter Explanations

### Temperature Parameters

#### Box 1 Temperature (T₁) — Equatorial

**Physical Meaning**: The **fixed** surface temperature of Box 1 (equatorial), representing the Atlantic equatorial region (~5–20°N).

**Default**: 25°C (Stommel's original)

**Typical Range**: 15–35°C (realistically 20–28°C)

**Real-world context**: Atlantic equatorial sea surface temperature is typically 25–27°C. This is *held constant* during a simulation, representing fast atmospheric equilibration.

**What it does**:
- **Larger T₁** (e.g., 30°C): Increases thermal gradient ΔT = T₁ − T₂ → stronger thermal drive → AMOC is more stable and harder to tip
- **Smaller T₁** (e.g., 20°C): Reduces ΔT → weaker thermal drive → AMOC is more fragile → lower ΔT_crit

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing T₁ **raises ΔT_crit** → larger temperature difference needed before collapse
- Decreasing T₁ **lowers ΔT_crit** → easier to trigger collapse via ΔT reduction

**How to use**: Adjust T₁ to explore how equatorial warming affects AMOC stability.

**Validation constraint**: T₁ > T₂ (enforced by the model)

#### Box 2 Temperature (T₂) — Polar

**Physical Meaning**: The **fixed** surface temperature of Box 2 (polar), representing high latitudes (>50°N in the North Atlantic, including Nordic Seas and Labrador Sea).

**Default**: 5°C (Stommel's original)

**Typical Range**: -2°C (near freezing/sea ice) to 25°C (very warm, unrealistic)

**Real-world context**: Nordic Seas/Labrador Sea surface temperature ranges from -2°C (winter, with sea ice) to ~8°C (summer). Present-day ≈ 5°C on average.

**What it does**:
- **Lower T₂** (e.g., 0°C): Increases ΔT = T₁ − T₂ → stronger thermal drive → AMOC more stable
- **Higher T₂** (e.g., 15°C): Decreases ΔT → weaker thermal drive → AMOC more fragile → approaching collapse

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Lowering T₂ → increases ΔT → raises ΔT_crit (moves away from tipping)
- Raising T₂ → decreases ΔT → lowers ΔT_crit (approaches tipping)

**Physical interpretation**: **Polar amplification** (high-latitude warming faster than tropics) is modeled by increasing T₂ while keeping T₁ constant. This is a key climate risk: polar warming reduces ΔT and pushes AMOC toward the critical threshold.

**How to use**:
- **Explore present-day**: T₁=25°C, T₂=5°C → ΔT=20°C (robust, far from tipping)
- **Explore past glacials**: T₁=25°C, T₂=0°C → ΔT=25°C (strong AMOC)
- **Explore future warming**: T₁=27°C, T₂=10°C → ΔT=17°C (approaching fragility)
- **Find the tipping point**: Slowly adjust T₂ upward until ΔT ≤ ΔT_crit (use bifurcation diagram to find exact threshold)

**Validation constraint**: T₂ < T₁ (enforced)

### Salinity Parameters

In the **original Stommel relaxation model**, salinity evolves according to:

$$\frac{dS_i}{dt} = -\lambda(S_i - S_i^0) + q(S_{j} - S_i)$$

There are **two salinity components per box**:
1. **Equilibrium salinity** ($S_i^0$): The relaxation target, representing long-term climatological values
2. **Initial salinity** ($S_i^{\text{init}}$): The starting value at t=0, allowing exploration of different initial conditions

#### Box 1 Equilibrium Salinity (S₁ᵉᵍ)

**Physical Meaning**: The long-term equilibrium (relaxation target) salinity for Box 1 (equatorial region), set by atmospheric forcing.

**Default**: 36 psu (Stommel's original)

**Typical Range**: 30–40 psu (physically 35–37 psu)

**Real-world context**: Tropical Atlantic surface salinity is typically 35–37 psu. Higher values represent net evaporation; lower values represent net precipitation.

**What it does**:
- The relaxation term $-\lambda(S_1 - S_1^0)$ pulls Box 1 salinity toward this target over ~1000 years
- **Larger S₁ᵉᵍ** (e.g., 37 psu): Higher equilibrium salinity for equatorial box (more evaporative)
- **Smaller S₁ᵉᵍ** (e.g., 35 psu): Lower equilibrium salinity (more precipitation)

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Affects the equilibrium salinity difference ΔS⁰ = S₁ᵉᵍ − S₂ᵉᵍ
- Changes the size of the bistable region and the location of ΔT_crit

**How to use**: Adjust S₁ᵉᵍ to represent different climatic regimes (wet tropics vs. dry tropics).

#### Box 2 Equilibrium Salinity (S₂ᵉᵍ)

**Physical Meaning**: The long-term equilibrium (relaxation target) salinity for Box 2 (polar region), set by atmospheric forcing and ice sheet dynamics.

**Default**: 34 psu (Stommel's original)

**Typical Range**: 28–40 psu (physically 32–35.5 psu)

**Real-world context**: North Atlantic polar surface is typically 33–34.5 psu. Ice melt and freshwater input can reduce this. High values represent saltier polar water.

**What it does**:
- The relaxation term $-\lambda(S_2 - S_2^0)$ pulls Box 2 salinity toward this target
- **Larger S₂ᵉᵍ** (e.g., 34.5 psu): Higher equilibrium salinity (less freshwater input)
- **Smaller S₂ᵉᵍ** (e.g., 33 psu): Lower equilibrium salinity (more freshwater/ice melt)

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- ΔS⁰ = S₁ᵉᵍ − S₂ᵉᵍ determines the equilibrium salinity difference
- Larger ΔS⁰ makes AMOC more stable (easier to maintain salt gradient)
- Smaller ΔS⁰ makes AMOC more fragile

**How to use**: Lower S₂ᵉᵍ to represent periods with high freshwater input (Greenland melt, ice sheet discharge).

#### Box 1 Initial Salinity (S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ)

**Physical Meaning**: The salinity in Box 1 at t=0 (start of simulation).

**Default**: 36 psu

**Typical Range**: 30–40 psu

**What it does**:
- Determines the initial condition in phase space
- System evolves from this starting point toward an attractor (upper or lower branch)
- Initial salinity difference ΔS(t=0) = S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ − S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ is crucial

**Effect on simulation**:
- **Large S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ** (e.g., 37 psu): Large initial ΔS → tends toward upper (strong AMOC) branch
- **Small S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ** (e.g., 34 psu): Small initial ΔS → may approach lower (collapsed) branch

**How to use**: Adjust S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ (with S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ) to explore different initial conditions and demonstrate bistability:
- Set S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ=36, S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ=34 (large ΔS) → evolves to upper branch
- Set S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ=34.5, S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ=34 (small ΔS) → evolves to lower branch

#### Box 2 Initial Salinity (S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ)

**Physical Meaning**: The salinity in Box 2 at t=0.

**Default**: 34 psu

**Typical Range**: 28–40 psu

**What it does**:
- Determines the initial polar salinity and thus the initial ΔS
- Very low S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ (e.g., ≤32 psu) can initialize the system in a freshened, collapsed state

**Effect on simulation**:
- Initial condition in phase space; system evolves from here

**How to use**: Pair with S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ to create different bistable scenarios. In bistable region (ΔT > ΔT_crit), small differences in initial ΔS can lead to different equilibria.

### Physical Coefficients (Stommel's Original Values)

The circulation strength is given by: $q = k[\alpha \Delta T - \beta \Delta S]$

#### Thermal Expansion (α)

**Physical Meaning**: The rate at which seawater density decreases with temperature increase.

**Default**: 2 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹ (**Stommel's original value**)

**Typical Range**: 0.5–3.0 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹

**Real-world context**: Typical seawater at 20°C has α ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹. The value is temperature-dependent but this constant approximation is standard in oceanography.

**What it does**:
- Appears in the thermal driving term of circulation: q = k[**α·ΔT** − β·ΔS]
- Larger α → stronger effect of temperature on density → stronger thermal drive → AMOC more stable, harder to tip
- Smaller α → weaker thermal sensitivity → easier to collapse via ΔT reduction

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing α → raises ΔT_crit (larger ΔT needed before collapse)
- Decreasing α → lowers ΔT_crit (easier to trigger collapse)

**How to use**: α is typically fixed at Stommel's value. Vary it to explore sensitivity to thermal feedback strength.

**Validation**: Stommel's original experiments used α = 2×10⁻⁴; this is the "best-fit" value for thermohaline dynamics.

#### Haline Contraction (β)

**Physical Meaning**: The rate at which seawater density increases with salinity increase.

**Default**: 1 × 10⁻³ psu⁻¹ (**Stommel's original value**)

**Typical Range**: 2–15 × 10⁻⁴ psu⁻¹

**Real-world context**: Typical seawater has β ≈ 7.6 × 10⁻⁴ psu⁻¹, but Stommel used 1×10⁻³ for his idealized two-box model.

**What it does**:
- Appears in both thermal and haline terms: q = k[α·ΔT − **β·ΔS**]
- Larger β → salinity differences have bigger effect on density → haline feedback is stronger → easier to collapse
- Smaller β → salinity has weaker effect → AMOC more resilient

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing β → lowers ΔT_crit (easier to tip)
- Decreasing β → raises ΔT_crit (harder to tip)

**Interpretation**: β is the **"fragility" parameter**. A larger β means the ocean is more sensitive to salinity variations because salt has a strong density effect. This is why freshwater forcing (which reduces salinity) is so dangerous.

**How to use**: β is typically fixed at Stommel's value. Vary to explore how salinity's density effect changes the bifurcation structure.

#### Circulation Coefficient (k)

**Physical Meaning**: An empirical coupling constant that converts density differences into circulation strength (units: inverse time).

**Default**: 3 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹ (**Stommel's original value**)

**Typical Range**: 1 × 10⁻¹⁰ to 1 × 10⁻⁶ s⁻¹ (logarithmic scale)

**Real-world context**: Stommel calibrated k to match observed AMOC strength (~15–20 Sv); modern GCMs use various values depending on resolution and parameterization.

**What it does**:
- q = **k**[α·ΔT − β·ΔS]
- Larger k → same density difference produces faster/stronger circulation (higher q)
- Affects the *magnitude* and *timescale* of circulation response

**Effect on bifurcation diagram** (important!):
- Increasing k → affects the **absolute magnitude** of q but NOT the critical ΔT (bifurcation threshold is independent of k!)
- The equilibrium ΔS values DO depend on k, but the critical ΔT where branches collide does not

**Why k doesn't affect ΔT_crit**: The critical condition from the relaxation model depends on k, but both the thermal and haline terms scale with k, so the bifurcation point is independent of k's absolute value.

**How to use**:
- Keep at default (Stommel's 3×10⁻⁹) for canonical two-box behavior
- Increase k to see faster dynamics (reaches equilibrium quicker)
- Decrease k for slower dynamics (longer transients)

**Simulation behavior**: k affects integration timescale — larger k means faster evolution to equilibrium.

#### Relaxation Rate (λ)

**Physical Meaning**: The rate at which salinity relaxes toward its equilibrium value, representing long-timescale ocean-atmosphere interaction.

**Default**: 3 × 10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹ (**Stommel's original value**)

**Corresponding Equilibration Timescale**: τ = 1/λ ≈ **1000 years**

**Typical Range**: 1 × 10⁻¹² to 1 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹ (logarithmic scale)

**Real-world context**: The ~1000-year timescale represents:
- Ocean mixing and diffusion of salinity anomalies
- Slow adjustment of the thermocline to surface forcing
- Paleoclimate observations suggest D-O events and glacial-interglacial cycles involve ~1000-year timescales

**Physical Interpretation**: Salinity doesn't instantly adjust to the atmosphere. Rather, freshwater (or salt deficit) anomalies persist and decay slowly via ocean mixing and advection. The term $H_i = -\lambda(S_i - S_i^0)$ in the ODEs implements this.

**What it does**:
- Controls how fast salinity "forgets" its initial condition and returns to equilibrium
- Larger λ (shorter τ) → faster relaxation → system adjusts quickly to equilibrium
- Smaller λ (longer τ) → slower relaxation → system is sluggish, remembers initial conditions longer

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing λ → raises ΔT_crit (faster relaxation makes AMOC more stable)
- Decreasing λ → lowers ΔT_crit (slower relaxation makes system more fragile)

**Why λ matters for climate risk**:
- If the real ocean has shorter relaxation timescale (larger λ, faster mixing), AMOC is more resilient
- If relaxation is slow (small λ), salinity anomalies persist, making AMOC vulnerable to prolonged forcing

**How to use**:
- Default (3×10⁻¹¹, ~1000 years): Canonical Stommel model
- Increase λ to explore faster ocean adjustment scenarios
- Decrease λ to explore slow-response scenarios (relevant for deep ocean processes)

### The Bifurcation Parameter: Temperature Difference (ΔT)

**In the original Stommel (1961) relaxation model, the KEY bifurcation parameter is the temperature difference:**

$$\Delta T = T_1 - T_2$$

**Not** freshwater flux (F) as in simplified models!

#### Understanding ΔT_crit: The Critical Temperature Difference

**Physical Meaning**: ΔT_crit is the threshold temperature difference below which the upper (strong AMOC) branch ceases to exist. When ΔT < ΔT_crit, the system is forced into the collapsed state.

**Estimated Value** (from Stommel's original parameters): ΔT_crit ≈ **10–15°C**

**Present-day Earth**: ΔT ≈ **20°C** (T₁ = 25°C, T₂ = 5°C) → **Robust and far from tipping** ✓

**How to Find ΔT_crit**:
1. Click **"Compute Bifurcation Diagram"**
2. Look for the **red diamond** (bifurcation point) on the diagram
3. Read the ΔT value at that point on the x-axis
4. That is ΔT_crit for your chosen parameters

#### Effect on Bifurcation and Tipping

**What happens as you adjust T₁ and T₂ (thus changing ΔT)**:

- **Large ΔT** (e.g., 25°C): Two stable branches exist (upper and lower) — **bistable region**
  - Upper branch: strong, thermally-driven AMOC
  - Lower branch: weak or collapsed
  - System's fate depends on initial conditions (history)

- **Small ΔT** (e.g., ΔT ≈ ΔT_crit, maybe 12°C): Branches approach each other
  - Upper branch is weakening and becoming less stable
  - System shows critical slowing down (slower recovery from perturbations)
  - Risk of noise-induced tipping increases

- **Very small ΔT** (ΔT < ΔT_crit, e.g., 10°C): Upper branch **vanishes**
  - Only the lower (collapsed) branch remains
  - Strong AMOC is impossible — **system must collapse**

#### Paleoclimate and Future Scenarios

**Glacial Period Example** (Last Glacial Maximum, ~20,000 years ago):
- Colder polar regions → lower T₂ (e.g., 0°C)
- Similar or slightly cooler equator → similar T₁
- Result: ΔT ≈ 20–25°C (or potentially higher)
- **Paradox**: Glacial AMOC should be strong, but evidence suggests frequent collapse (D-O events)
- **Resolution**: Bistability at glacial ΔT allows noise-induced transitions without forcing parameter change

**Future Warming Scenario** (Representative Concentration Pathway 8.5, year 2100):
- Arctic warming faster than tropics (polar amplification)
- T₁ may increase to 27°C, T₂ to 10°C
- Result: ΔT decreases to ~17°C (still above ΔT_crit for default parameters)
- But approaching fragility region; closer to bifurcation → higher tipping risk

#### How to Explore Tipping with Temperature

**Experiment 1: Find the Critical Point**
1. Set T₁ = 25°C, T₂ = 5°C (present-day, ΔT = 20°C)
2. Run simulation: observe strong AMOC (upper branch)
3. Click "Compute Bifurcation": find ΔT_crit
4. Gradually increase T₂ until ΔT approaches ΔT_crit
5. Watch AMOC weaken (critical slowing down)
6. Cross ΔT_crit: AMOC collapses

**Experiment 2: Bistability**
1. Set ΔT > ΔT_crit to ensure bistability
2. Start simulation with S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ = 36, S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ = 34 (large ΔS): → evolves to upper branch (strong AMOC)
3. Run again with S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ = 34.5, S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ = 34 (small ΔS): → evolves to lower branch (weak AMOC)
4. Same ΔT, same parameters, but different outcomes (bistability demonstrated!)

**Experiment 3: Noise-Induced Tipping in Bistability**
1. Set ΔT in bistable region (ΔT > ΔT_crit), but close to critical value (ΔT ≈ ΔT_crit + 3)
2. Set initial condition on upper branch (strong AMOC)
3. Run with noise = 0: AMOC stays on upper branch
4. Run with noise = 3×10⁻⁵: occasionally jumps to lower branch (stochastic tipping)
5. Run multiple times with same noise level: some runs collapse, some don't (probabilistic tipping)

### Integration Parameters

#### Integration Time (t_max)

**Physical Meaning**: The total length of time the simulation runs, in years.

**Default**: 3000 years

**Typical Range**: 100–10,000 years

**What it does**:
- Determines how long you wait for the system to reach steady state
- Longer simulations are more expensive computationally but reveal long-term behavior

**When to adjust**:
- **Near tipping**: Increase to 5000–10,000 years to see slow approaches to bifurcation
- **Quick exploration**: Decrease to 500–1000 years for fast iterations (system usually equilibrates within 1000 years for far from bifurcation)
- **Noise experiments**: May need longer to see multiple transitions

**Default rationale**: 3000 years is sufficient for the system to equilibrate in most regimes, matching paleoclimate timescales.

#### Output Sampling Interval (dt)

**Physical Meaning**: The time step at which results are *output* and plotted.

**Default**: 1.0 year

**Typical Range**: 0.1–100 years

**IMPORTANT**: This is a *diagnostic* parameter only. The internal ODE solver uses adaptive time stepping (Radau method) and does not depend on dt. Changing dt:
- Does **not** affect accuracy
- Does **not** change the simulation results
- **Only** affects the resolution of output time series

**When to adjust**:
- **Smooth plots**: Decrease dt to 0.1 year for finer time series resolution
- **Quick output**: Increase dt to 10–100 years if you only care about final state (faster data download)

**Default rationale**: 1 year per point gives good time series plots without excessive data.

#### Noise Amplitude (σ)

**Physical Meaning**: Standard deviation of Gaussian white noise added to the ODEs.

**Default**: 0 (no noise)

**Typical Range**: 0 to 5 × 10⁻⁵ psu/s

**What it does**:
- When σ = 0: Purely deterministic system (reproducible results)
- When σ > 0: Gaussian noise is added at each integration step
  - q → q + σ·ξ(t) where ξ(t) is Gaussian white noise
  - System becomes stochastic; each run gives different trajectory

**Physical interpretation**: Noise represents:
- Unresolved sub-grid variability (eddies, mesoscale processes)
- Weather noise (storms, atmospheric variability)
- Decadal climate modes (NAO, AMO)

**Effect on simulation**:
- **No noise (σ = 0)**: System follows deterministic trajectory to attractor. Near bifurcation, tiny perturbations decay or grow exponentially (marginal stability).
- **Small noise (σ = 1–2 × 10⁻⁵)**: System fluctuates around attractor; occasional small excursions
- **Moderate noise (σ = 3–5 × 10⁻⁵)**: Regular fluctuations; may see noise-induced transitions across basin boundary
- **Large noise**: Transitions become very frequent; signal drowns in noise

**Noise-induced tipping**: In the bistable region (ΔT > ΔT_crit), even if the system deterministically stays on the upper branch, large enough noise can occasionally kick the system across the unstable manifold to the lower (collapsed) branch. This is **stochastic tipping** — a key mechanism for paleoclimate transitions like D-O events.

**How to observe it**:
1. Set ΔT in bistable region, close to ΔT_crit (e.g., ΔT = ΔT_crit + 3°C)
2. Initialize on upper branch (S₁ᵢⁿⁱᵗ large, S₂ᵢⁿⁱᵗ small)
3. Run with σ = 0: stays on upper branch (deterministic)
4. Run with σ = 3–5×10⁻⁵: occasionally jumps to lower branch (stochastic tipping)
5. Run multiple times with same noise: probability of tipping increases near critical point

**When to use**:
- Set σ > 0 to explore how noise affects stability
- Compare deterministic (σ=0) and stochastic (σ>0) simulations to quantify robustness
- Study noise-induced tipping by running same parameters 5–10 times and watching for transitions
- Understand paleoclimate variability: noise can explain D-O event-like oscillations

## Parameter Constraints and Validation

The simulator enforces the following constraints:

| Constraint | Formula | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| T₁ > T₂ | Always | Equator must be warmer than pole |
| α, β, k, λ > 0 | Always | Physical coefficients must be positive |
| S₁, S₂ can be any value | Flexible | Allows exploration of fresh and salty states |

**Note on salt conservation**: The model does **not** enforce total salt conservation; instead, both boxes have independent relaxation dynamics $H_i = -\lambda(S_i - S_i^0)$ toward their respective equilibrium values. This is consistent with Stommel's original formulation where salinity relaxes toward atmospheric equilibrium targets.

If you violate a constraint (e.g. set T₁ ≤ T₂), the API returns a **422 Validation Error** with a helpful message.

## Bifurcation Diagram Reading Tips

When you compute a bifurcation diagram (sweeping T₂ to vary ΔT), the critical temperature difference depends on parameters:

$$\Delta T_{\text{crit}} = \frac{\sqrt{8k\beta\lambda\Delta S^0} - \lambda}{2k\alpha}$$

**How the red diamond (bifurcation point) moves:**

- **Increase α** (thermal expansion): ΔT_crit **decreases** (thermal drive stronger, harder to collapse)
- **Increase β** (haline contraction): ΔT_crit **increases** (salinity effect stronger, easier to collapse)
- **Increase k** (circulation strength): ΔT_crit changes slightly (non-monotonic dependence)
- **Increase λ** (faster relaxation): ΔT_crit **decreases** (relaxation stabilizes, harder to collapse)
- **Increase ΔS⁰** (equilibrium salinity difference): ΔT_crit **increases** (larger salt gradient to maintain, harder without strong circulation)

**Use this intuition** to predict where the bifurcation point will move, then recompute to verify!
