## Detailed Parameter Explanations

### Temperature Parameters

#### Equatorial Temperature (T_e)

**Physical Meaning**: The fixed surface temperature of the equatorial box, representing the Atlantic equatorial region (~5–20°N).

**Default**: 25°C

**Typical Range**: 15–35°C (but realistically 20–28°C)

**Real-world context**: Atlantic equatorial sea surface temperature is typically 25–27°C.

**What it does**:
- **Larger T_e** (e.g., 30°C): Increases thermal gradient ΔT = T_e − T_p → stronger thermal drive → AMOC is more stable and harder to tip
- **Smaller T_e** (e.g., 18°C): Reduces ΔT → weaker thermal drive → AMOC is more fragile

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing T_e **shifts critical F higher** → larger freshwater forcing needed to cause tipping
- Decreasing T_e **lowers critical F** → easier to tip

**Validation constraint**: T_e > T_p (enforced by the model)

#### Polar Temperature (T_p)

**Physical Meaning**: The fixed surface temperature of the polar box, representing high latitudes (>50°N in the North Atlantic).

**Default**: 5°C

**Typical Range**: -2°C (near freezing/sea ice) to 15°C (unusual warm extremes)

**Real-world context**: Nordic Seas and Labrador Sea surface temperature ranges from -2°C (winter, with sea ice) to ~8°C (summer).

**What it does**:
- **Lower T_p** (e.g., 0°C): Increases ΔT → stronger thermal drive → AMOC more stable
- **Higher T_p** (e.g., 10°C): Decreases ΔT → weaker thermal drive → AMOC more fragile

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**: Same as adjusting T_e in opposite direction
- Lower T_p → higher critical F
- Higher T_p → lower critical F

**Physical interpretation**: In a warming climate, polar regions warm faster than the equator (polar amplification). Higher T_p models this effect and makes tipping easier.

**Validation constraint**: T_p < T_e (enforced)

### Salinity Parameters (Initial Conditions)

#### Equatorial Salinity (S_e0)

**Physical Meaning**: The initial salinity in the equatorial box at t=0.

**Default**: 36 psu

**Typical Range**: 30–40 psu (physically 35–37 psu)

**Real-world context**: Atlantic equatorial surface salinity is 35–37 psu. High values can represent net evaporation zones.

**What it does**:
- **Larger S_e0** (e.g., 37 psu): More salt in equatorial box initially → larger initial ΔS → starts closer to upper branch
- **Smaller S_e0** (e.g., 35 psu): Less salt → smaller initial ΔS → may start closer to lower branch

**Effect on simulation**:
- Initial salinity determines initial condition in phase space
- System relaxes toward its attractor (upper or lower branch) depending on which basin of attraction S_e0 is in
- In bistable region, can "switch" between stable states by changing S_e0

#### Polar Salinity (S_p0)

**Physical Meaning**: The initial salinity in the polar box at t=0.

**Default**: 34 psu

**Typical Range**: 28–40 psu (physically 32–35 psu)

**Real-world context**: North Atlantic polar surface is fresher than equatorial due to ice melt and freshwater input; typical range 33–34.5 psu.

**What it does**:
- **Larger S_p0** (e.g., 35 psu): Smaller initial ΔS → starts on upper branch (AMOC-like)
- **Smaller S_p0** (e.g., 33 psu): Larger initial ΔS but with opposite sign → may start on lower branch or collapsed regime

**Effect on simulation**:
- Initial ΔS = S_e0 − S_p0 determines starting point
- Very low S_p0 (≤30 psu) can initialize the system in the collapsed state

**Physical interpretation**: Representing freshwater input by starting with lower polar salinity is a diagnostic tool. Real freshwater forcing evolves over time (parameter F), not just via initial conditions.

### Physical Coefficients

#### Thermal Expansion (α)

**Physical Meaning**: The rate at which seawater density decreases with temperature increase.

**Default**: 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ /°C

**Typical Range**: 0.5–3.0 × 10⁻⁴ /°C

**Real-world context**: Typical seawater at 20°C has α ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁴ /°C. The value is temperature-dependent but this constant approximation is standard.

**What it does**:
- Appears in thermal driving: q = k[α·ΔT − β·ΔS]
- Larger α → stronger effect of temperature on density → stronger thermal drive → more stable AMOC

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing α → shifts critical F *higher* (larger F needed to tip)
- Decreasing α → shifts critical F *lower* (easier to tip)

**Physically meaningful range**: 0.5–3.0 × 10⁻⁴ represents different water masses and temperatures.

#### Haline Contraction (β)

**Physical Meaning**: The rate at which seawater density increases with salinity increase.

**Default**: 8 × 10⁻⁴ /psu

**Typical Range**: 2–15 × 10⁻⁴ /psu

**Real-world context**: Typical seawater has β ≈ 7.6 × 10⁻⁴ /psu (nearly constant over oceanographic ranges).

**What it does**:
- Appears in both thermal and haline terms: q = k[α·ΔT − β·ΔS]
- Larger β → salinity differences have bigger effect on density → haline feedback is stronger → easier to trigger collapse

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- Increasing β → shifts critical F *lower* (easier to tip)
- Decreasing β → shifts critical F *higher* (harder to tip)

**Physically meaningful range**: 2–15 × 10⁻⁴ captures variation across different water masses.

**Interpretation**: β is the "fragility" parameter. A larger β means the ocean is more sensitive to freshwater forcing because salinity variations have a bigger density effect.

#### Circulation Coefficient (k)

**Physical Meaning**: An empirical coupling constant that converts density differences into circulation strength.

**Default**: 1.5 × 10⁻⁶ /s

**Typical Range**: 5 × 10⁻⁸ to 1 × 10⁻⁵ /s (logarithmic scale)

**What it does**:
- q = k[α·ΔT − β·ΔS]
- Larger k → same ΔT difference produces faster circulation
- Affects *rate* of approach to equilibrium but *not* the equilibrium value itself (at steady state, F = q·ΔS is independent of k if q is scaled appropriately)

**Effect on bifurcation diagram**:
- **Paradoxically, k affects neither equilibria nor critical F** (in the equilibrium sense)
- Instead, k affects the **timescale**: larger k → faster dynamics → reaches steady state sooner
- But note: the critical F formula involves k: $F_c = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta}$, so k does affect the bifurcation threshold!

**Simulation behavior**:
- Use a coarse dt to capture dynamics with small k
- Use a fine dt to resolve fast dynamics with large k

**Default calibration**: Chosen so that with default parameters, q_sv ≈ 14.7 Sv, matching RAPID observations.

### Freshwater Forcing (The Key Bifurcation Parameter)

#### Freshwater Flux (F)

**Physical Meaning**: Net freshwater input (or removal) to the polar box, in salinity units.

**Default**: 1.0 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s

**Typical Range**: 0 to 5 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s

**Physical interpretation**: F > 0 means net freshwater *input* to polar box (dilutes it):
- Glacial melt from Greenland ice sheet
- Excess precipitation minus evaporation at high latitudes
- Melting sea ice (releases freshwater)
- Arctic river discharge

**What it does**: This is the **primary bifurcation parameter**
- Small F: AMOC is strong and stable (upper branch)
- F approaches F_c1: AMOC weakens gradually
- F exceeds F_c1: AMOC collapses (jump to lower branch)
- Very large F: AMOC stays collapsed

**Hysteresis behavior**:
- Forward tipping at F_c1 ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s
- Backward recovery at F_c2 ≈ 1 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s
- Bistable region: 1 × 10⁻⁴ < F < 2 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s

**Real-world context**:
- Present-day Greenland mass loss: ~280 Gt/year ≈ 0.3 × 10⁻⁴ psu/s (very roughly)
- The model suggests we're not yet at the tipping point (F < F_c1), but freshwater input is increasing

**Experimentation**: This slider is the most important for exploring tipping points and hysteresis. Try:
1. Set F to 0.8 × 10⁻⁴ (strong AMOC)
2. Gradually increase to 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ (collapse)
3. Decrease back to 0.5 × 10⁻⁴ (watch for hysteresis recovery at lower F than collapse)

### Integration Parameters

#### Integration Time (t_max)

**Physical Meaning**: The total length of time the simulation runs, in years.

**Default**: 3000 years

**Typical Range**: 100–10,000 years

**What it does**:
- Determines how long you wait for the system to reach steady state
- Longer simulations are more expensive computationally but reveal long-term behavior

**When to adjust**:
- **Near tipping**: Increase to 5000–10,000 years to see slow approaches to bifurcation
- **Quick exploration**: Decrease to 500–1000 years for fast iterations (system usually equilibrates within 1000 years for far from bifurcation)
- **Noise experiments**: May need longer to see multiple transitions

**Default rationale**: 3000 years is sufficient for the system to equilibrate in most regimes, matching paleoclimate timescales.

#### Output Sampling Interval (dt)

**Physical Meaning**: The time step at which results are *output* and plotted.

**Default**: 1.0 year

**Typical Range**: 0.1–100 years

**IMPORTANT**: This is a *diagnostic* parameter only. The internal ODE solver uses adaptive time stepping (Radau method) and does not depend on dt. Changing dt:
- Does **not** affect accuracy
- Does **not** change the simulation results
- **Only** affects the resolution of output time series

**When to adjust**:
- **Smooth plots**: Decrease dt to 0.1 year for finer time series resolution
- **Quick output**: Increase dt to 10–100 years if you only care about final state (faster data download)

**Default rationale**: 1 year per point gives good time series plots without excessive data.

#### Noise Amplitude (σ)

**Physical Meaning**: Standard deviation of Gaussian white noise added to the ODEs.

**Default**: 0 (no noise)

**Typical Range**: 0 to 5 × 10⁻⁵ psu/s

**What it does**:
- When σ = 0: Purely deterministic system (reproducible results)
- When σ > 0: Gaussian noise is added at each integration step
  - q → q + σ·ξ(t) where ξ(t) is Gaussian white noise
  - System becomes stochastic; each run gives different trajectory

**Physical interpretation**: Noise represents:
- Unresolved sub-grid variability (eddies, mesoscale processes)
- Weather noise (storms, atmospheric variability)
- Decadal climate modes (NAO, AMO)

**Effect on simulation**:
- **No noise (σ = 0)**: System follows deterministic trajectory to attractor. Near bifurcation, tiny perturbations decay or grow exponentially (marginal stability).
- **Small noise (σ = 1–2 × 10⁻⁵)**: System fluctuates around attractor; occasional small excursions
- **Moderate noise (σ = 3–5 × 10⁻⁵)**: Regular fluctuations; may see noise-induced transitions across basin boundary
- **Large noise**: Transitions become very frequent; signal drowns in noise

**Noise-induced tipping**: In the bistable region, even if F < F_c1 (deterministically no tipping), large enough noise can occasionally kick the system to the lower branch. Rerun the same parameters multiple times with noise to see stochastic variability.

**When to use**:
- Set σ > 0 to explore how noise affects stability
- Compare deterministic (σ=0) and stochastic (σ>0) simulations to quantify robustness
- Study noise-induced tipping by running same parameters 5–10 times and watching for transitions

## Parameter Constraints and Validation

The simulator enforces the following constraints:

| Constraint | Formula | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| T_e > T_p | Always | Equator must be warmer than pole |
| S_e + S_p = constant | Enforced | Salt conservation |
| All parameters > 0 | Enforced | Physical quantities must be positive |

If you violate a constraint (e.g., set T_e < T_p), the simulator returns a **422 Validation Error** with a helpful message.

## Bifurcation Diagram Reading Tips

When you compute a bifurcation diagram, the tipping points depend on parameters:

$$F_{c1} = \frac{k(\alpha\Delta T)^2}{4\beta} \cdot f(\text{stability})$$

where f(·) is a factor accounting for stability. Roughly:

- **Increase ΔT** (e.g., lower T_p): F_c shifts *right* (need more F to tip)
- **Increase β**: F_c shifts *left* (tip more easily)
- **Increase k**: F_c shifts *right* (stabilizing)
- **Increase α**: F_c shifts *right* (stabilizing)

Use this intuition to predict how the S-curve will move when you adjust a parameter, then recompute to verify!
